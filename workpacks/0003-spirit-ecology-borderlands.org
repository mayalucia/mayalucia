* Context and Motivation

MāyāLucIA uses two parallel registers to describe the same structural
insights: system vocabulary (spirits, harnesses, guilds, powers) and
story vocabulary (devtas, kunds, gurs, thread walkers). The translation
table in =aburaya/powers/story-compose.md= maps between them. But the
story side of that table is thin — drawn mostly from Kullu valley
practice, specifically the devta system of the Parvati and Tirthan
valleys. This is a narrow slice of a vast mountain range.

The Western Himalaya — from the Pamirs through Karakoram, Ladakh,
Zanskar, Spiti, Kinnaur, Kullu, Kangra, and into the Nepal Himalaya —
sustains one of the densest and most varied spirit ecologies on Earth.
These traditions are not decorative. They encode:

- *Environmental knowledge*: which springs are sacred (and therefore
  protected), where avalanches come, how water behaves at altitude
- *Social contracts*: how communities negotiate with forces beyond
  their control — weather, disease, trade collapse, imperial invasion
- *Psychological models*: trance, possession, divination as diagnostic
  instruments for individual and collective distress
- *Spatial knowledge*: passes, trade routes, seasonal migration
  patterns, territorial boundaries marked by spirit jurisdiction

A spirit tradition is a *measurement instrument*. The human psyche,
shaped by millennia of inhabiting these mountains, registers phenomena
that no satellite measures: the quality of a silence before snowfall,
the character of a gorge, the social memory of a trade route. The
stories that encode these measurements are data — not metaphor.

** Three uses

This knowledge base serves three concrete purposes:

1. *Story composition*: The sutradhar-guardian composes stories set
   across the transborder Himalaya. It needs to know what kinds of
   spirits inhabit each region, what their customs are, what vocabulary
   the traditions use. Currently it draws from Kullu alone.

2. *Agent ontology*: Aburaya's spirit typology (currently just
   "guardian spirit / devta") needs enrichment. The review spirit
   discussion surfaced the need for wandering spirits (jogini), flow
   spirits (nāg devta), specialist spirits (bajantri). These are not
   arbitrary names — they should be grounded in actual traditions,
   with the functional mapping earned, not imposed.

3. *Digital twin of the mountain range*: Parbati is a digital twin of
   the Parvati valley. But the vision is larger — the whole Western
   Himalaya as an interconnected system of valleys, passes, watersheds,
   trade routes, and human settlements. The spirit ecology is one data
   layer in that twin: it encodes human-landscape interaction at a
   depth that geophysical data alone cannot reach.

** What this is not

This is not a folkloristics PhD. It is a working cartographer's survey:
what spirits exist where, what they do, what they tell us about the
landscape. Sufficient depth for story composition and agent naming.
Not sufficient depth for ethnographic publication. We are users of this
knowledge, not its originators — cite sources, respect the traditions,
acknowledge what we do not understand.

** In the literature

Key sources to consult (non-exhaustive — the agent executing this WP
should expand through research):

*** Kullu and inner Himalaya

- Penelope Chetwode, /Kulu: The End of the Habitable World/ (1972) —
  devta processions, temple architecture, the rath system
- M. Postel, A. Neven & K. Mankodi, /Antiquities of Himachal/ (1985)
- Colin Rosser, ethnography of Kullu villages (1950s fieldwork)
- Madhu Ramnath, /Woodsmoke and Leaf Cups/ — Baiga and Himalayan
  forest ecology (the sacred grove as conservation instrument)
- Local documentation: the 534 devtas of Kullu district, the rath
  (chariot) festival system, the gur possession protocol

*** Kinnaur and Spiti

- Kinnauri nāg worship — serpent spirits associated with lakes and
  springs at high altitude
- Spiti Buddhist-Bön synthesis — spirit propitiation in a nominally
  Buddhist landscape
- The Kinnaur-Tibet borderland: Nako, Tabo, and the trade route spirits

*** Ladakh and Zanskar

- The lha (mountain gods) and klu (water spirits, cognate with nāga)
- Oracles (lha-mo, lha-pa) — trance possession in Tibetan Buddhist context
- John Crook & Henry Osmaston, /Himalayan Buddhist Villages/ (1994)
- Kim Gutschow, /Being a Buddhist Nun/ — Zangskar women's practices

*** Tibet and the roof

- The yul-lha (country gods, territorial spirits of landscape features)
- The btsan (warrior spirits of red rocks and cliffs)
- The sa-bdag (earth lords, lords of the soil — disturbed by digging)
- Bön traditions: pre-Buddhist spirit taxonomy, the mountain as axis
- Rene de Nebesky-Wojkowitz, /Oracles and Demons of Tibet/ (1956) —
  the most comprehensive Western survey of Tibetan spirit taxonomy
- Samten Karmay on Bön ritual texts

*** Karakoram and Pamirs

- The peri tradition (Persian-influenced spirit ecology in Hunza, Gilgit)
- Fairies of the high pastures — spirits associated with altitude
  sickness, glacier behaviour, and avalanche
- Lorimer's work on Burushaski folklore
- The Wakhi and Kyrgyz traditions of the Pamir corridor

*** Nepal Himalaya (eastern extension)

- The jhākri (shaman) tradition — spirit diagnosis and healing
- Bombo drumming and trance — the Nepal equivalent of the Kullu gur
- Mountain spirits of Solu-Khumbu, Langtang, Annapurna

*** Cross-cutting themes

- Sacred geography: how spirit jurisdictions map to watershed boundaries
- The hot spring (kund) as liminal space across all traditions
- Possession as diagnostic instrument: what the trance measures
- Trade route spirits: guardians of passes, caravan protectors
- The relationship between spirit taxonomy and altitude zones
  (valley floor → alpine → glacial → trans-Himalayan desert)

* Specification (What You Build)

** Output: =domains/epistem/surveys/spirit-ecology.org=

An Org-mode knowledge base. This is the authoritative survey — the
source of truth from which WP-0004's YAML gazetteer extracts are
derived. It lives in epistem (the knowledge domain) rather than
parbati (the Parvati valley twin), because the survey spans the
entire transborder Himalaya.

Structure:

*** Part 1: Regional Spirit Taxonomies

For each region (Kullu, Kinnaur, Spiti, Ladakh, Zanskar, Tibet/Bön,
Karakoram/Pamirs, Nepal), document:

1. *Spirit types* — what categories of supernatural beings are
   recognised? Use the local vocabulary. Do not flatten into a
   universal taxonomy.
2. *Landscape associations* — which spirits are associated with
   which landscape features? (peaks, passes, springs, gorges,
   forests, glaciers, trade routes)
3. *Human interfaces* — how do humans interact with these spirits?
   (oracles, mediums, ritual specialists, festivals, prohibitions)
4. *Diagnostic functions* — what do these traditions measure?
   What information about the environment, the community, or the
   individual does the spirit system encode?

*** Part 2: Cross-Regional Patterns

Identify patterns that recur across regions:

1. *Altitude zonation* — how does the spirit ecology change with
   altitude? (valley-floor agricultural spirits vs. high-pass
   guardian spirits vs. glacier/summit spirits)
2. *Water spirits across traditions* — nāg (Hindu), klu (Tibetan),
   peri (Persian-influenced). What is the underlying landscape
   logic?
3. *Possession/oracle traditions* — gur (Kullu), lha-pa (Ladakh),
   jhākri (Nepal), Bön mediums. What is the common diagnostic
   structure?
4. *Sacred geography as conservation* — how spirit jurisdictions
   protect springs, forests, pastures from over-exploitation.
   Documented cases.
5. *Trade route spirits* — spirits that guard passes and the spaces
   between territories. The liminal spirits.

*** Part 3: Functional Mapping for Aburaya

Map the surveyed spirit ecology to Aburaya's needs. This is where
the translation table gets extended. For each functional need:

- Guardian of a place (module/domain)
- Wanderer carrying a practice across places
- Flow spirit following a system (pipeline, data flow, watershed)
- Specialist performing a craft for any caller
- Medium through whom a spirit speaks (the harness relationship)
- Diagnostic spirit (evaluation, review, critique)

Identify which real traditions provide the best source analogy.
Propose vocabulary. Note where the analogy is strong and where it
breaks — we are cartographers, not theologians.

*** Part 4: Story-Compose Extension

Update the translation table and geographic reference for story
composition:

- Extend the existing Miyazaki → Himalaya key with new entries
  from the surveyed traditions
- For each valley/region, list the spirit vocabulary available
  for stories set there
- Identify 3-5 new story settings enabled by the expanded
  knowledge (e.g. a story set in Ladakh can now use lha and
  klu vocabulary; a Pamir story can use peri traditions)

*** Part 5: Digital Twin Data Layer

Propose how the spirit ecology integrates into the parbati digital
twin as a data layer:

- What is the data model? (GeoJSON features with spirit-type
  attributes? Org-mode gazetteer? Both?)
- What are the spatial primitives? (point: shrine/spring;
  line: trade route/river; polygon: spirit jurisdiction/watershed)
- What existing GIS data layers correlate? (watershed boundaries ≈
  spirit jurisdictions; hot spring locations ≈ kund/liminal spaces;
  pass elevations ≈ guardian spirits)
- What would a minimal prototype look like? (Parvati valley only,
  with devta jurisdictions overlaid on DEM)

** Format requirements

- All claims sourced. If from web research, include URL. If from
  a book, include author/title/page where possible.
- Use Org-mode throughout. Tables for comparisons. Headings for
  regions. =#+begin_quote= for direct quotations from sources.
- Do not romanticise. These are living traditions, not museum pieces.
  Where a practice is contested, endangered, or politically sensitive,
  note it.
- Respect the limits of web research. Where a topic requires fieldwork
  or access to sources not available online, say so. Mark gaps
  explicitly.

* What This Proves

That the project's parallel registers — system and story — can be
grounded in real geography and real tradition, not just literary
invention. That a digital twin of a mountain range includes the human
psyche as a measurement instrument. That the stories we tell about
spirits are data about the landscape, recorded by animals who have
inhabited it for millennia.

And practically: that an agent can do the cartographic survey,
producing a knowledge base dense enough for both story composition and
GIS data modelling. The human brings the interpretive frame; the agent
brings tireless traversal of sources.

* Open Questions

- How much depth per region? The Tibetan spirit taxonomy alone could
  fill a book. We need enough for story composition and agent naming,
  not an encyclopedia. The executing agent should aim for the level
  of detail in the existing Kullu material in =story-compose.md= —
  a working cartographer's sketch, not an ethnography.

- The politics of representation. These are living traditions in
  contested territories — Kashmir, Tibet, the Durand Line. The
  document should be scholarly in tone, not touristic. Where
  traditions are endangered or suppressed, note it without
  editorialising.

- Integration with the existing Miyazaki key. The Spirited Away
  mapping was a starting point. As the real traditions become richer,
  the Miyazaki layer may thin — the actual Himalayan spirit ecology
  is more interesting than the Japanese analogy. The key should evolve,
  not calcify.
