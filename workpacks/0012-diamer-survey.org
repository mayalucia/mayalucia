* Context and Motivation

The MāyāLucIA story series surveys the Western Himalaya through
found-document narratives. Twelve stories have covered the monsoon
valleys — Kullu, Parvati, Tirthan, Baspa, Spiti, Lahaul, Chandrabhaga,
Sutlej — their hydrology, geology, architecture, textiles, ecology,
and spirit traditions. All of these lie south and east of the main
Himalayan crest, within the monsoon's reach.

The Karakoram is different. North and west of the main range, beyond the
monsoon shadow, the landscape changes fundamentally: no deodar, no
terraced agriculture, no monsoon cycle. Glaciers dominate. The spirit
traditions shift from Hindu-Pahari devtas to Persian-Islamic peri and
Tibetan-influenced klu. The languages shift from Pahari to Shina and
Burushaski. The altitude is higher, the air drier, the rock different.

The Diamer region — named for the Nanga Parbat massif itself (*dia* =
spirit, *mer/mir* = mountain in Shina: "abode of spirits") — is where
the Himalaya, Karakoram, and Hindu Kush ranges converge. It is the
western anchor of the story world.

This WP is homework. Not a story, not a gazetteer entry — raw material.
Survey the region for what is interesting, what is surprising, what
creates tension. The story will find itself after the research is done.

** Why now

The gazetteer (WP-0004) populated two Karakoram genera —
=karakoram-peri= and =karakoram-bitan= — from the spirit ecology surveys.
These are thin entries compared to the Kullu devta or the Tibetan btsan.
The region deserves the same depth of attention the Tirthan Valley has
received across stories #7–#12. And there are tensions here that the
monsoon valleys don't carry:

- The mountain as "killer" vs the mountain as "abode of spirits" —
  the conquest narrative laid over the indigenous landscape
- Glacial agency — surging glaciers that advance kilometres in months,
  dam rivers, flood valleys. Not metaphorical agency; the glacier acts.
- The peri at altitude — spirit traditions that encode knowledge of
  altitude sickness, disorientation, and the behaviour of ice
- The colonial/modern overlay — German expeditions, speed records,
  tourism — a peculiar time for a timeless mountain

* Specification

** Part A: Physical geography

Survey and document:

1. *Geology* — the triple junction of Himalaya, Karakoram, Hindu Kush.
   Rock types, fault lines, the Nanga Parbat-Haramosh massif as a
   tectonic anomaly (fastest-rising mountain on Earth). Contrast with
   the gneiss and magnetite of the Kullu valleys.

2. *Glaciology* — Rakhiot, Rupal, Diamir glaciers. Surging behaviour.
   The Indus gorge at Nanga Parbat (deepest gorge on Earth). Glacial
   lake outburst floods (GLOFs). The glacier as agent.

3. *Ecology* — altitude zonation without monsoon. Juniper and birch
   instead of deodar. Alpine meadows (the =jūt= where peri dwell).
   Wildlife: ibex, markhor, snow leopard. The ecological contrast
   with the monsoon valleys.

4. *Hydrology* — the Indus as it passes Nanga Parbat. Tributaries.
   The river's character here vs the Sutlej or Tirthan. Irrigation
   in an arid landscape — contrast with kuhls.

** Part B: Spirit traditions and human culture

Survey and document:

1. *The peri tradition* — depth beyond the gazetteer entry. Named
   locations associated with peri. The relationship between peri and
   jinn in Shina/Burushaski cosmology. The bitan's trance practice.
   How peri-knowledge encodes altitude and glacier hazards.

2. *Languages* — Shina, Burushaski, Balti. Spirit vocabulary in each.
   The word =diamer= itself. Place-names that carry spirit-knowledge
   (Fairy Meadows = Jūt, etc.).

3. *Human settlement patterns* — Chilas, Rakhiot, the villages of the
   Indus gorge. Architecture in an arid high-altitude landscape.
   Agriculture without monsoon. Ismaili vs Sunni communities and
   their different relationships to pre-Islamic practice.

4. *The conquest narrative* — Mummery (1895), the German expeditions
   (1930s), Buhl (1953), Messner (1970), modern speed records. Not
   as story material per se, but as a layer the survey must
   acknowledge. The mountain's name in Western literature ("Killer
   Mountain") vs its Shina name ("abode of spirits"). The tension
   between these two stories *about the same place* is itself
   interesting.

5. *Contemporary life* — the KKH (Karakoram Highway), tourism,
   Fairy Meadows as destination, the Diamer-Basha Dam project.
   What is happening to the region now.

** Part C: Aesthetic register

What does this landscape look, sound, feel like?

- Light quality at altitude without monsoon haze
- Rock colours (contrast with Tirthan slate and Sutlej gneiss)
- Sound: wind, ice, absence of forest
- The visual drama of the Rupal Face (4,600m vertical)
- The aesthetic of aridity vs the lush monsoon valleys

This section is for the writer's eye — sensory vocabulary for future
stories.

** Part D: Narrative tensions (raw notes)

Collect tensions that could drive stories. Not story outlines —
tensions. Examples already identified:

- The spirit is not its entry (cataloguing vs knowing)
- The conquest narrative vs the inhabited mountain
- Glacial agency — the glacier as actor, not scenery
- Altitude sickness as spirit encounter
- The bitan who goes up to negotiate vs the mountaineer who goes up
  to conquer
- Arid landscape as absence (no monsoon, no deodar, no terraces) —
  what is present instead?

The human will add tensions from lived experience.
