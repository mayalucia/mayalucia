* Context and Motivation

CruVin (Commission C-001) is a wine education companion structured
around the WSET qualification levels 1–4. The site's MDX content
skeleton exists — 23 topic files across 4 levels — but the pages are
either empty or thinly populated. The guardian spirit needs raw
material: factual, sourced, structured knowledge about wine that it
can draw on when filling those pages.

This WP produces that raw material. It is the wine equivalent of the
Diamer surveys (WP-0012, WP-0013) — deep web research deposited as
a structured org file that the downstream builder consumes.

** Why this matters

The human is learning wine alongside the friend for whom CruVin is
being built. The survey is therefore also a learning scaffold: it
should explain /why/, not just /what/. Why does Pinot Noir express
differently in Burgundy and Oregon? Why does limestone matter? Why
does the timing of fortification determine sweetness? The facts must
carry their reasoning.

* Specification

** Part A: Grape Varieties and Vine Biology

*** A.1 Major grape varieties

For each of the following varieties, document: origin region, climate
preferences, flavour profile (primary/secondary/tertiary aromas),
key growing regions worldwide, classic food pairings, notable wines.

White: Chardonnay, Sauvignon Blanc, Riesling, Pinot Grigio/Gris,
Gewürztraminer, Viognier, Sémillon, Chenin Blanc, Albariño, Grüner
Veltliner, Muscat/Moscato.

Red: Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Pinot Noir, Syrah/Shiraz, Grenache,
Tempranillo, Sangiovese, Nebbiolo, Gamay, Zinfandel/Primitivo,
Malbec, Mourvèdre, Touriga Nacional, Carménère.

*** A.2 Vine biology and growing cycle

- The annual cycle: dormancy → budburst → flowering → fruit set →
  véraison → harvest. What happens physiologically at each stage.
- Photosynthesis, sugar accumulation, acid decline — the race to
  harvest.
- Vine age, yield, and quality relationship.
- Rootstocks and phylloxera: why nearly every vine in the world is
  grafted.

*** A.3 Climate, soil, and terroir

- Macro/meso/microclimate: definitions and examples.
- Growing degree days, diurnal temperature range, frost risk.
- Continental vs maritime vs Mediterranean climates.
- Key soil types and their effects: limestone (drainage + minerality),
  clay (water retention), gravel (heat + drainage), slate (heat
  retention), volcanic (mineral richness).
- The terroir concept: is it real? The scientific evidence for soil
  influence on flavour.

** Part B: Winemaking — Process and Science

*** B.1 White winemaking

Pressing → clarification → fermentation → aging → bottling.
Stainless steel vs oak. Malolactic conversion: chemistry and style
impact. Lees aging and bâtonnage.

*** B.2 Red winemaking

Destemming → crushing → fermentation with skins → pressing → aging.
Maceration time and tannin extraction. Whole-bunch fermentation.
Punch-down vs pump-over. Oak aging: French vs American, new vs old,
toast levels.

*** B.3 Sparkling wine methods

Traditional method (Champagne): second fermentation in bottle,
riddling, disgorgement. Tank/Charmat method (Prosecco). Transfer
method. Carbonation.

*** B.4 Fortified wine methods

Port: fermentation arrested by spirit addition (sweet). Sherry:
fermentation completed, then fortified (dry base); biological aging
(flor), oxidative aging, solera system. Madeira: estufagem.

*** B.5 Finishing

Fining (bentonite, egg white, vegan alternatives), filtration,
SO₂ management, closures (cork vs screw cap debate, evidence).

** Part C: Wine Regions of the World

For each major region, document: geography and climate, key
appellations/subregions, principal grape varieties, classification
system, notable producers, signature styles.

*** C.1 France

Bordeaux (Left Bank/Right Bank, 1855 classification, en primeur).
Burgundy (climat, cru system, négociants). Rhône (northern Syrah,
southern blends). Loire (Sancerre, Vouvray, Chinon). Alsace (Grand
Cru, vendange tardive). Champagne. Languedoc-Roussillon. Jura/Savoie.

*** C.2 Italy

Piedmont (Barolo, Barbaresco, Nebbiolo). Tuscany (Chianti Classico,
Brunello, Super Tuscans). Veneto (Amarone, Prosecco, Soave). Sicily.
Alto Adige/Trentino. Southern Italy.

*** C.3 Spain and Portugal

Rioja (crianza/reserva/gran reserva). Ribera del Duero. Priorat
(llicorella slate). Sherry (Jerez). Douro/Port. Vinho Verde. Madeira.
Alentejo.

*** C.4 Germany and Austria

Mosel, Rheingau, Pfalz (Prädikat system, VDP classification, the dry
Riesling revolution). Wachau (Grüner Veltliner, Smaragd). Burgenland.

*** C.5 New World

USA: Napa Valley, Sonoma, Oregon (Willamette Valley Pinot), Washington
State. Australia: Barossa Valley, Margaret River, Yarra Valley, Hunter
Valley, McLaren Vale. New Zealand: Marlborough (Sauvignon Blanc),
Central Otago (Pinot Noir), Hawke's Bay. Chile: Maipo, Casablanca,
Colchagua. Argentina: Mendoza (Malbec at altitude). South Africa:
Stellenbosch, Swartland, Constantia.

** Part D: Tasting, Evaluation, and Service

*** D.1 Systematic Approach to Tasting (SAT)

The WSET SAT methodology: appearance (clarity, intensity, colour),
nose (condition, intensity, aroma characteristics), palate (sweetness,
acidity, tannin, body, flavour intensity, finish). Quality assessment
framework.

*** D.2 Tasting vocabulary

Primary aromas (grape-derived), secondary (fermentation-derived),
tertiary (aging-derived). Building a useful vocabulary without jargon.
Common flavour families: citrus, stone fruit, tropical, red berry,
black fruit, floral, herbaceous, spice, earth, oak.

*** D.3 Food and wine pairing principles

Weight matching. Acidity and fat. Tannin and protein. Sweetness
matching. Regional pairing traditions. The science behind why certain
pairings work.

*** D.4 Wine faults

Cork taint (TCA). Oxidation. Reduction. Volatile acidity. Brettanomyces.
Heat damage. How to recognise each. When a "fault" is a stylistic choice.

** Part E: The Business and Culture of Wine

*** E.1 Classification and labelling

EU system (PDO/PGI). French AOC. Italian DOCG/DOC/IGT. Spanish DO.
German Prädikat. New World varietal labelling. Reading a wine label.

*** E.2 Wine economics

Supply chain: vineyard → winery → négociant → distributor → retailer.
En primeur. Auction market. Fine wine as investment. Bulk wine vs
estate bottled. Cooperative vs independent.

*** E.3 Contemporary issues

Climate change: shifting growing zones, earlier harvests, rising
alcohol, new varieties. Sustainability: organic, biodynamic, natural
wine (definitions, evidence, debates). Water use. Carbon footprint
(shipping vs local). Diversity and inclusion in the wine industry.

*** E.4 Wine history (brief)

The Caucasus origins (~6000 BCE). Greek and Roman viticulture.
Monastic winemaking in medieval Europe. Phylloxera crisis (1860s–).
New World expansion. The 1976 Judgment of Paris. Natural wine movement.

That the survey → content pipeline works for a commission project.

(this WP) from presentation (cruvin-guardian's build work) mirrors
the Diamer pattern: one spirit surveys, another (or the same) builds.

* Open Questions

- How much Level 4 (Diploma) material should this survey cover?
  L4 topics (fortified, sparkling, world wines in exhaustive detail)
  are substantially deeper. The survey might flag L4 as "structure
  only, detail deferred" rather than attempting comprehensive coverage.
- WSET's own textbooks are not freely available. The survey draws on
  publicly available wine education resources (Wine Folly, Guild of
  Sommeliers, Jancis Robinson, academic viticulture sources). Flag
  any topic where public sources are thin.
- The friend's tasting experience: which wines have they tried?
  This affects which examples resonate. Deferred to human annotation.
