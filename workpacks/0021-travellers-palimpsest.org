* Context and Motivation

The Diamer surveys (WP-0012, WP-0013) built the physical and spiritual
landscape of the Nanga Parbat massif — geology, glaciology, ecology,
petroglyphs, spirit traditions. But the mountain has also been a
*destination* across millennia. People have walked, ridden, climbed,
and died here — each seeing a different mountain through their own
frame.

This WP surveys the *human journeys* to Diamer: who came, when, why,
what they wrote, and what happened to them. The output is raw material
for a story — tentatively titled "The Travellers to Diamer" — that
takes the form of a guest register spanning 10,000 years. Each entry
is a different voice, a different century, a different reason for
being at the foot of the mountain that builds itself.

** Why this matters

The stories translate system concepts into valley language. The concept
this story translates: *the same infrastructure seen by different
agents, each reading it through their own model*. The mountain is
the infrastructure. The travellers are the agents. The frame they
bring — Buddhist, Mughal, colonial, mountaineering, touristic,
scientific — is the model. The mountain doesn't change. The visitor's
reading does.

This is also the first story set in territory the human cannot
physically visit. The border makes Diamer inaccessible. But the
*voices* of people who went there are accessible — in Chinese pilgrim
accounts, Mughal court records, British expedition memoirs, Czech
Alpine Club reports. The story is built from other people's journeys,
not our own. That constraint is itself part of the narrative.

** What makes this survey different

WP-0012 and WP-0013 were *regional* surveys — what exists in this
place. This WP is a *diachronic* survey — who came here across time.
The organising axis is not geography but chronology. Each era
contributes a layer to the palimpsest.

* Specification

** Part A: Ancient travellers (~8000 BCE – 700 CE)

For each known journey or presence, document: who, when, why they came,
what they wrote or left behind, primary sources with URLs.

*** A.1 The petroglyph makers

- Who were the earliest carvers? Hunter-gatherers from where?
- Evidence of long-distance movement: Okunev culture connection
  (Siberia to Indus, Bronze Age)
- What the carving styles tell us about origin communities

*** A.2 Chinese Buddhist pilgrims

- *Faxian* (~399 CE): the /s\'sa\d{n}kupatha/ (suspended crossing) in
  the Indus gorge. His own account from /A Record of the Buddhist
  Kingdoms/. The peg-path technique. What he saw, what terrified him.
- *Dharmodgata*: contemporary of Faxian, different account of the
  same crossing
- *Xuanzang* (~630 CE): his account of the Indus. The capsized
  manuscript boat. What Buddhist sites he visited en route.
- Any other named Chinese pilgrim who passed through
- Source: Harry Falk's paper on the /s\'sa\d{n}kupatha/ (Academia.edu)

*** A.3 Sogdian, Persian, and other Silk Road travellers

- The Sogdian inscriptions at Thalpan — who were these merchants?
  What were they trading? Where were they going?
- Aramaic/Hebrew inscriptions (disputed) — if verified, who left them?
- Any Persian accounts from the Sassanid or earlier period?

*** A.4 The Buddhist period summit (~100 BCE – 700 CE)

- Who built the monasteries and carved the Jataka scenes at Thalpan?
- The density of Buddhist inscriptions — was this a major pilgrim route?
- When and why did Buddhist presence end in Diamer?

** Part B: Medieval travellers (700–1800 CE)

*** B.1 Arab geographers and travellers

- Any references to Diamer/Nanga Parbat in Arab geographical works?
  Al-Biruni (11th c.) surveyed the subcontinent — did he mention
  the northern mountains?
- Ibn Battuta (1333): his Indus crossing. How close did he come to
  Diamer? What did he see?

*** B.2 Central Asian and Afghan rulers

- Diamer under Tibetan influence, then local Shina-speaking kingdoms
- Any account from the rulers of Gilgit? The Trakh\aacute{}n dynasty?
- The spread of Islam in Diamer — when, how, by whom?

*** B.3 Mughal connections

- Dara Shikoh: is the fort at Chilas real? Primary sources?
  Dara Shikoh the Upanishad translator — what was a Mughal prince
  doing at the edge of the empire?
- Mughal administrative records mentioning Gilgit, Chilas, or
  the northern frontier
- Abu'l Fazl's /A\'in-i-Akbar\'i/ — any reference to these regions?

*** B.4 Sikh and Dogra campaigns

- Zorawar Singh's invasion of Baltistan (1839–40). His route. What
  the Dogra army encountered.
- Nathu Shah in Gilgit — installed a ruler, killed by Mir of Hunza.
  The story of his death.
- The region's transition from Sikh empire to British influence via
  the Treaty of Amritsar (1846).

** Part C: Colonial and mountaineering era (1800–1947)

*** C.1 Early European arrivals

- Who were the first Europeans to see Nanga Parbat? The Great
  Trigonometrical Survey?
- G.T. Vigne (1835–38) — first European in Baltistan/Gilgit area?
- Godwin-Austen and the survey expeditions

*** C.2 Mummery (1895)

- The full account: who was Mummery? Who were Ragobir Thapa and
  Goman Singh? What did J. Norman Collie write about the loss?
- The porters' perspective — any recorded account from the Gurkha
  side?
- How Mummery's death shaped mountaineering history

*** C.3 The German obsession (1930s–1950s)

- 1932 Merkl reconnaissance
- 1934 disaster: 10 dead. Nazi funding. 600 local porters. The
  porter experience — what did local Chilas men think of the Germans?
- 1937 catastrophe: 16 dead in one avalanche. No survivors in camp.
  The decision to return after 1934 — politics of "the mountain of
  German destiny"
- 1938: "enlisting porters caused anxiety due to talk of evil spirits
  and devils" — who recorded this? Full context.
- Buhl (1953): the solo summit. His own account. The 17-hour ascent.
  What he saw from the top.
- The /Schicksalsberg/ narrative — Nanga Parbat as national project.
  How the Nazi regime instrumentalised the mountain.

*** C.4 British colonial administration

- The Gilgit Agency: British political agents in the region. Any
  memoirs? D.L.R. Lorimer collected Burushaski while serving as
  Political Agent (1920–24).
- The Gilgit Scouts. The 1947 uprising.

** Part D: Modern era (1947–present)

*** D.1 Messner and the post-war mountaineers

- Reinhold Messner, Nanga Parbat (1970): the Rupal Face first ascent.
  G\uuml{}nther's death. The 35-year controversy. The boot emerging from
  the glacier. DNA confirmation. Messner's memoir /The Naked Mountain/.
- Later notable ascents and tragedies

*** D.2 The 2013 massacre

- 10 climbers + 1 guide killed at base camp by militants. The first
  attack on climbers in Gilgit-Baltistan. Original target was
  kidnapping, not murder. The aftermath — foreign climbers vanished
  from Pakistan's mountains for years.

*** D.3 Kl\'ara Kolouchov\'a (2025)

- First Czech woman to summit Everest, K2, Kangchenjunga. Fell
  between Camp 1 and Camp 2 on Nanga Parbat, July 3, 2025. Age 46.
  The Sherpa Taraman Tamang was with her. What happened.

*** D.4 The KKH and tourism

- The Karakoram Highway (1959–1979): how it changed access to Diamer.
  Ranked second deadliest highway. The jeep road to Fairy Meadows —
  15 km, width of one jeep, 1,000m drop.
- Tourism: who visits now? Pakistani domestic tourists? Trekkers?
  The Fairy Meadows tourism boom. Social media and the mountain.

*** D.5 The dam workers

- Diamer-Basha Dam: the people building it now. Where do they come
  from? What do they think about the petroglyphs they're drowning?
  Any published accounts, journalism, or documentary coverage?

** Part E: The voices never heard

*** E.1 Women of Diamer

- The old women of Rupal who know the peri stories. The /pari khan/
  tradition (Chitral) — women as spirit mediums. Any documented
  women's accounts from the region?

*** E.2 Porters and guides

- The local Chilas men who carried loads for German expeditions.
  The Sherpas who died on the mountain. Any porter memoirs or
  oral histories?
- Contemporary porter/guide accounts from tourism

*** E.3 Love stories and folk romance

- Heer Ranjha is Punjabi, not Shina. Does the Shina oral tradition
  have its own love stories? Lorimer's Burushaski folktales — any
  romances?
- Any equivalent of /laila majnun/ or /sohni mahiwal/ from the
  northern mountains?

*** E.4 Local oral tradition

- The /gap/ identified in WP-0012 section H.3: no comprehensive
  collection of oral histories from Diamer district exists in
  published form. What fragments exist? Lok Virsa collections?
  ROSHAN publications? PhD theses?

* What This Proves

That a mountain can be surveyed not just as a place but as a
*destination* — a palimpsest of arrivals, each reading the same
landscape through a different frame. The Feynman imperative applied
to human geography: understand the mountain by reconstructing who
came to it and what they saw.

Also: that a story can be set in a place the authors cannot visit.
The constraint is real (the border exists). But the voices of those
who went there are preserved in text — Chinese, Persian, Urdu,
German, English, Czech. The story is built from those voices, not
from our own footsteps. The limitation becomes the form.

* Open Questions

- How deep is the Arab geographical tradition on the northern
  mountains? Al-Biruni is the obvious starting point, but there
  may be others.
- Did Marco Polo actually pass through or near Diamer? Or only
  through the broader Karakoram? His route is debated.
- Are there any Persian-language accounts (not just references)
  of the Diamer region? Babur's /Baburnama/ describes his campaigns
  in detail — does it reach this far north?
- The Diamer-Basha Dam: is there any published journalism from
  the workers' perspective? This is the most contemporary voice
  and perhaps the hardest to find.
- Shina love stories: Lorimer's Burushaski collections (1935) are
  the obvious source. Are they available online?
