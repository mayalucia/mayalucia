* Potocki Structural Analysis

Compiled from web research on /Manuscrit trouvé à Saragosse/
(1797–1815) and secondary sources.

** Nesting Mechanics

Potocki achieves 8 levels of nesting at maximum depth (P.N. Furbank's
"eightfold /emboîtement/"). The mechanisms:

1. *Formulaic handoff*: "Here is my story" — narration shifts to
   first person ("I") for the new narrator. No typographic
   differentiation. No quotes, no indentation, no italics. The shift
   is purely grammatical.

2. *The pronoun trap*: "virtually every 'I' is a mad summons to
   retrace your way back through tale-told jungles" (Believer
   Magazine). The reader must track which "I" is speaking. Potocki
   provides no external cues beyond narrative logic.

3. *Day boundaries as reset points*: "The Nth Day" returns the reader
   to Level 1 (Alphonse's journal). Each day begins with waking,
   eating, then storytelling resuming. The daily reset prevents total
   disorientation.

4. *"X continued his story"*: Brief transition phrases mark re-entry
   into nested frames. Only breadcrumbs.

5. *Physical interruption as frame exit*: Meals, sleep, arrivals,
   supernatural events — force the narrative back up through levels.

** Adaptation to our novella

For ~20,000 words, 3 levels of nesting is the maximum. Our
route-book structure already provides the "day boundary" reset — each
route-book is a chapter-level return to the Thread Walker frame.

Within each route-book:
- L0 = the traveller's daily observations
- L1 = stories told by people met on the road
- L2 = stories within those stories (the grandmother's song, the
  stone-cutter's teacher's technique)

The *pronoun trap* translates to voice differentiation: the Trader's
ledger voice, the Pilgrim's devotional voice, the Shepherd's seasonal
fragments. No typographic markers needed — the voice IS the marker.

** The Gomelez Technique (retroactive reframing)

In Potocki, apparently disconnected stories resolve into a hidden
genealogical conspiracy. The Gothic trials of the early days are
retroactively explained as theater.

In our novella, apparently disconnected route-books resolve into a
hidden ecological connection. The three routes are retroactively
revealed as one landscape seen from three altitudes. *Not a
conspiracy but an ecology.* The distinction matters: Potocki's
characters were being manipulated. Our travellers are not. They are
simply three people walking the same mountain without knowing it.

The technique is the same (plant connections, reveal late) but the
meaning is different (diversity as ecology, not diversity as
conspiracy).

** The Found-Document Frame

Potocki opens with a French officer finding a manuscript in a besieged
inn. *He never returns to this frame.* The non-closure is deliberate
— every explanation is potentially just another story.

Our adaptation: the Thread Walker finds route-books in a serai. She
reads them. She records connections in her own notebook. But she does
not explain or close the frame. The reader assembles the ecology.

** The Comedy Question

Potocki manages six tonal registers: Gothic horror, erotic/libertine,
picaresque comedy, philosophical, mathematical comedy, and adventure.
Tonal shifts are managed through *narrator switch* — each narrator
brings their own register.

For us: the Trader brings dry factual humour (the observation of
absurdity without commenting on it). The Pilgrim brings earnest
devotion that tips into the comic when she records dreams as matter
of fact. The Shepherd brings no humour — her fragments are too
sparse for comedy. The Thread Walker's frame voice can carry the
ironic distance that Potocki achieves through his Enlightenment
cosmopolitanism.


* Geography

** Jalori Pass (3,120 m)

Connects Inner Seraj (Kullu side) to Outer Seraj (Shimla/Sutlej
side). The pass is flanked by Jalori and Bashleo (3,250 m). Bashleo
separates Inner from Outer Seraj.

Below the pass on the Kullu side: Shoja, Jibhi, Banjar, the Tirthan
valley. Below on the Seraj side: Ani, Khanag, Nirmand, the road to
Rampur on the Sutlej.

** The Three Routes

*** Route 1: Kullu → Spiti (The Trader)

Kullu → Naggar → Manali → Marhi → Rohtang (3,978 m) → Khoksar →
Lahaul (Chandra valley) → Batal → Kunzum (4,550 m) → Losar → Kaza.

The transition at Rohtang: from green Kullu to brown Lahaul. The
glacier-melt rivers change colour — turquoise on the south, grey on
the north. A geological and visual boundary.

*** Route 2: Jalori → Kinnaur (The Pilgrim)

Jalori → Shoja → Ani → Nirmand → Rampur Bushahr → Sarahan →
Sangla → Kalpa → Chitkul.

Nirmand: wooden temples, Kath-Kuni construction. Sarahan: Bhimakali
temple. Chitkul: last village on the Indo-Tibetan border. The
Hindu-Buddhist transition happens along this route — Hindu temples in
Nirmand, syncretic temples at Sarahan and Sangla, Buddhist influence
at Chitkul.

Rampur Bushahr: gateway to Kinnaur, on the left bank of the Sutlej.
Former winter capital of the Bushahr kingdom. The Lavi Fair — ancient
trade between India and Tibet.

*** Route 3: The Seraj high pastures (The Shepherd)

Above Khanag → Baga Sarahan meadows → ridge between Beas and Sutlej
watersheds → below Bashleo Pass.

The Shepherd does not cross Bashleo. She grazes the ridge. From her
meadows she can see into both valleys. The watershed divide is subtle
here — two streams that flow in opposite directions (one north to
the Beas, one south to the Sutlej) may originate within a few hundred
metres of each other.

Shangarh meadow (Sainj valley): sacred grassland, Shangchul Mahadev
temple, Kath-Kuni. Nearby but distinct from the Shepherd's territory
(the Shepherd is above Khanag, not in the Sainj).

** Kath-Kuni Architecture

Traditional Himachali construction: alternating layers of wood
(deodar) and stone masonry, no mortar. Nail-less framework that
flexes with seismic waves. The air gap between double-skinned walls
dissipates both thermal and seismic energy. A thousand years old.
Transmitted orally through apprenticeship.

Relevant for: the Pilgrim's observations at Nirmand and Sarahan,
the Shepherd's hut construction.

** Cultural Context

- Kullu shawl weaving: fine pashmina, the /natti/ patterns
- Stone carving: tradition spanning from Nirmand to Chitkul
- GHNP (Great Himalayan National Park): UNESCO World Heritage,
  adjacent to Tirthan valley. Western tragopan (jujurana), musk deer,
  snow leopard
- Bushahr kingdom: trade treaty with Tibet, Lavi Fair, horses
  and swords exchanged
- The Hindu-Buddhist syncretism of Kinnaur: Kamru Fort with Buddha
  at the main gate, Bering Nag temple, wooden pagoda temples
