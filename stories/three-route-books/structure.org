#+title: Three Route-Books — Nesting Structure
#+author: mu2tau + sūtradhar
#+date: 2026-03-13

* Outer Frame: The Thread Walker at Jalori

The Thread Walker arrives at a serai near the top of Jalori Pass in
late autumn — the last weeks before snow closes the pass. The keeper
is an old woman who has maintained the serai for decades. Unlike
the keeper at the Tirthan–Sainj junction (who sorts tiles by identity),
this keeper collects route-books — the small bazaar notebooks that
travellers sometimes leave behind, full or half-full, when they
descend to the other side.

The keeper does not read them. She cannot read. But she sorts them
by route — she knows, from the condition of the notebook (mud type,
altitude staining, the wear pattern on the cover), which valley the
traveller came from and which direction they were heading.

She gives the Thread Walker three notebooks from the same season —
one from the Kullu side heading to Spiti, one from the Seraj side
heading to Kinnaur, one that never left the high pastures between.
"These three," she says, "walked the same year. None of them knew
the others."

The Thread Walker reads them over three evenings by lantern light.

** Frame markers

Entry: "From the first route-book:" / "From the second route-book:"
Exit: The Thread Walker's notebook entries between books, reflecting
on what she has read.

The frame returns between each route-book and at the end.

* Route-Book One: The Trader

** Voice: Ledger

Short sentences. Quantities. Distances. Weather observations.
Prices at each stop. Road conditions. The trader writes for
herself — to remember what she paid, what she carried, what the
road was like. She does not write for a reader.

** Route: Kullu → Manali → Rohtang → Lahaul → Kunzum → Spiti

*** Day entries (sample structure)

**** Day 1: Kullu to Naggar
- Load: 4 sacks salt (Mandi bazaar rate), 2 bundles Kullu shawls
- Road: paved to Naggar, then mule track
- Weather: clear, cold morning, warm by midday
- Observation: the apple orchards above Naggar are still in leaf

**** Day 3: Manali to Marhi
- The road narrows. Mules uneasy.
- Met a group of Lahauli traders coming down. They say Rohtang
  is passable but "only just" — snow on the north face, ice in
  the morning.

**** Day 5: Crossing Rohtang (3,978 m)
- The crossing. Wind from the north.
- [NESTED STORY — Level 1]: The muleteer tells the story of his
  grandmother, who crossed Rohtang seventy times and could read
  the weather from the colour of the lichen on the stones near
  the top. "Green means go. Yellow means wait. Red means turn
  back." His grandmother composed a song about the crossing —
  [Level 2]: the song itself, four stanzas, naming landmarks.
  The last stanza names a place: "the meadow where the two
  waters almost meet." The Trader does not know what this means.
  The reader will recognise it later as the Shepherd's pasture.

*** Hidden connections planted in Book One

1. The muleteer's name: Ratan. (He appears as a reference in
   Book Two — the stone-cutter mentions "my brother Ratan who
   takes the salt road.")
2. The grandmother's song names "the meadow where the two waters
   almost meet" — this is the Shepherd's summer pasture.
3. A Kullu shawl the Trader carries has a particular pattern —
   "the pattern they call /natti/, the one that follows the river."
   This pattern appears on a weaving in the Shepherd's account.
4. At Kunzum, the Trader shelters in a stone enclosure and finds
   a carved mark — a figure she cannot identify. The Pilgrim will
   later describe the same mark at a shrine in Kinnaur, explaining
   its meaning.

* Route-Book Two: The Pilgrim

** Voice: Devotional diary

Flowing sentences. Shrine descriptions. Dreams recorded as matter
of fact — "I dreamed that the goddess stood at the waterfall and
her hair was the water." Vows made and kept (or broken — the
Pilgrim is honest about what she did not do). The Pilgrim writes
as witness: she records what she sees at shrines because she
believes the seeing itself is the devotion.

** Route: Jalori → Ani → Nirmand → Rampur → Sarahan → Kalpa → Chitkul

*** Day entries (sample structure)

**** Day 1: Descent from Jalori to Shoja
- The forest below the pass. Deodar, then oak, then rhododendron.
- First shrine: a small stone figure at the trailside, dressed
  in red cloth. She leaves a coin.

**** Day 3: Nirmand
- The wooden temple. Kath-Kuni construction — alternating stone
  and timber, no mortar. She describes the earthquake resistance
  as a metaphor for faith: "the building that bends does not
  break."
- [NESTED STORY — Level 1]: The stone-cutter at Nirmand tells
  her about his training. His teacher was from Kinnaur, where
  the tradition crosses from Hindu carving to Buddhist carving.
  "The chisel does not know the difference," the stone-cutter
  says. He mentions his brother — "Ratan, who takes the salt
  road" — and says his brother carries a different kind of
  knowledge: "he reads the weather. I read the stone."
  [Level 2]: The stone-cutter describes a pattern he learned
  from his teacher — a carved figure that appears at shrines
  from Nirmand to Chitkul. It is the same figure the Trader
  found at Kunzum.

**** Day 7: Sarahan
- The Bhimakali temple. The wooden tower. The view of Shrikhand
  Mahadev (5,227 m).
- She dreams of a meadow she has never visited — "high, between
  two ridges, where the grass was the colour of old gold and a
  woman was weaving." This is the Shepherd.

*** Hidden connections planted in Book Two

1. The stone-cutter's brother is Ratan — the muleteer from Book One.
2. The carved figure at Nirmand shrines = the mark at Kunzum.
3. The Pilgrim's dream of the weaving woman = the Shepherd.
4. At Sarahan, the Pilgrim records a song sung at the temple —
   its melody, she says, "follows the river the way a fish
   follows the current." A variation of the muleteer's
   grandmother's song from Book One, but with different words
   and a Kinnauri melody. Same structure, different tradition.
5. The Pilgrim mentions "the master list of shrines kept at
   Nirmand" — it lists four traditions of carvers. A fifth has
   been working the upper temples for two seasons without
   appearing in any list. (Translates: the stale guild list
   from WP-0037.)

* Route-Book Three: The Shepherd

** Voice: Seasonal fragments

Not full sentences — lists, observations, notes to self. Grass
quality rated by colour and height. Weather recorded by what the
animals do (not by instruments). The Shepherd writes for her own
future seasons: "September: the meadow above Khanag. Grass still
good. Water from the north stream. The sheep prefer the east-facing
slope in the afternoon."

The Shepherd does not think of herself as a traveller. She does
not cross passes. She moves within a territory she knows so well
that she does not name landmarks — they are "the rock," "the
stream," "the place where the monal sits in the morning."

** Route: The high pastures between Beas and Sutlej watersheds

The Shepherd moves between summer pastures above Khanag, Baga
Sarahan, the meadows below Bashleo Pass. She does not cross
Bashleo into the Sutlej drainage — she grazes the ridge. From her
meadows, she can see into both valleys but belongs to neither.

*** Entry structure (seasonal, not daily)

**** Early autumn: Above Khanag
- Grass turning. Sheep moving to lower slopes.
- The loom in the hut. She weaves in the evenings.
  The pattern is /natti/ — "the one my grandmother taught me,
  the one that follows the river."
- [NESTED STORY — Level 1]: Her grandmother's account of how
  the /natti/ pattern was designed: it follows the course of
  the Tirthan from its source to the junction with the Sainj.
  Each bend in the river is a bend in the pattern. The weaver
  who designed it walked the river and translated the walk into
  thread. [Level 2]: The original weaver's name — the same name
  as the muleteer's grandmother from Book One. They are the same
  person, seen from two traditions: the muleteer remembers her
  song, the Shepherd remembers her weaving. Different media,
  same source.

**** Mid-autumn: Below Bashleo
- The meadow narrows. Two streams converge — one flowing north
  (toward the Beas), one flowing south (toward the Sutlej).
  "The place where the two waters almost meet." She has always
  known this place. She does not know it has a name in a song
  she has never heard.
- A pilgrim passed through — "a woman walking alone, with a
  notebook." The Shepherd gave her water but they did not
  speak much. The pilgrim asked about a dream she had had.
  The Shepherd told her: "you dreamed of me."

*** Hidden connections planted in Book Three

1. The /natti/ pattern = the Trader's shawl pattern. Same
   design, originating from the same weaver.
2. "The meadow where the two waters almost meet" = the place
   named in the muleteer's grandmother's song (Book One).
3. The grandmother who composed the song (Book One) is the
   grandmother who designed the weaving pattern (Book Three).
   Song and weaving are two records of the same landscape —
   one auditory, one textile.
4. The pilgrim who passed through the meadow is the Pilgrim
   from Book Two. The Shepherd saw her. The Pilgrim dreamed of
   the Shepherd. Neither understands the other's account.
5. The loom the Shepherd inherited — she does not say from whom,
   but the description matches the loom at Nahin from "The
   Weaver's Loom" (story #11). The tradition is continuous.

* The Resolution (Thread Walker's final notebook entry)

The Thread Walker, having read all three route-books over three
evenings, writes:

She has found:
- The muleteer and the stone-cutter are brothers (Book 1 + Book 2)
- The grandmother is one person remembered in two traditions:
  as a singer (muleteer) and as a weaver (Shepherd)
- "The meadow where the two waters almost meet" is a real place
  known to the Shepherd, named in a song known to the muleteer,
  and dreamed of by the Pilgrim
- The carved figure at Kunzum (Book 1) is the same figure
  described at Nirmand (Book 2) — same tradition, different
  altitudes
- The Pilgrim passed through the Shepherd's territory without
  knowing it; the Shepherd saw the Pilgrim without knowing her
  route-book existed

The resolution is ecological: the three routes are one landscape
seen from three altitudes. The Trader sees the passes. The Pilgrim
sees the valleys. The Shepherd sees the meadows between them.
Together they describe a single watershed system — but none of them
sees the whole.

The Thread Walker does not explain this. She records it. The reader
assembles the ecology from the Thread Walker's juxtaposition.
