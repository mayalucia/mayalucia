Three Route-Books

a composition companion

↓

A route-book is not a diary. A diary records what the writer feels. A route-book records what the road offers: the gradient, the weather, the state of the bridges, the price of dal at the last dhaba, the bird that appeared on the stone wall at the turn above the gorge.


A diary faces inward. A route-book faces the trail.


Read them, the keeper said. You are the first person to hold all three at once.

The First Route-Book

Day 1. Kullu to Naggar. Load: 4 sacks rock salt, Mandi bazaar rate, 14 rs/kg. 2 bundles Kullu shawl — the fine weave, not the tourist grade. Total weight with pack frame: 38 kg on the lead mule, 32 on the second.

Day 5. Rohtang. The crossing. 3,978 metres. Wind from the north — steady, not gusting, the kind of wind that does not surprise you but does not stop.

Day 9. Kunzum. The pass. 4,550 metres. Below us, Spiti opened. Brown, vast, dry as a bone.

The rock salt will sell.

The Second Route-Book

Day 1. Descent from Jalori to Shoja. The forest below the pass. Deodar, then oak, then rhododendron. First shrine: a small stone figure at the trailside, dressed in red cloth. I left a coin.

Day 3. Nirmand. The wooden temple. Kath-Kuni construction — alternating stone and timber, no mortar. The building that bends does not break.

Day 7. Sarahan. The Bhimakali temple. I dreamed of a meadow I have never visited — high, between two ridges, where the grass was the colour of old gold and a woman was weaving.

I will walk to Chitkul.

The Third Route-Book

September. The meadow above Khanag. Grass still good. Water from the north stream. The sheep prefer the east-facing slope in the afternoon.

The loom in the hut. The pattern is natti — the one my grandmother taught me, the one that follows the river.

A pilgrim passed through — a woman walking alone, with a notebook. I gave her water but we did not speak much. She asked about a dream she had had.

I told her: you dreamed of me.

What the Thread Walker Sees
i
The first evening, after reading the Trader's notebook —
Trader → Shepherd
The muleteer Ratan told her a song his grandmother composed. The song names a place: “the meadow where the two waters almost meet.” The Trader writes it down because the words have “the precision of a place name.” She does not know where the meadow is. Book One, Day 3 · above Marhi
A song about a place she has never seen The Shepherd has always known this place. She does not know it has a name in a song she has never heard.
ii
Still the first evening —
Trader → Pilgrim
At Kunzum, the Trader finds a carved mark inside a stone enclosure. Not Tibetan script, not Devanagari, not any writing she has seen on this road. She sketches it in the margin. Book One, Day 9 · Kunzum Pass, 4,550 m
A figure she cannot identify — sketched at 4,550 m The stone-cutter at Nirmand will describe this same figure. The same tradition, carried to a different altitude.
iii
The second evening, after reading the Pilgrim's notebook —
Pilgrim → Trader
The stone-cutter at Nirmand mentions his brother — “Ratan, who takes the salt road.” He says his brother carries a different kind of knowledge: “he reads the weather. I read the stone.” Book Two, Day 3 · Nirmand, 1,800 m
“my brother Ratan who takes the salt road” Ratan the muleteer, who walked with the Trader for an hour above Marhi and told her his grandmother's song.
iv
Still the second evening —
Pilgrim → Shepherd
At Sarahan the Pilgrim dreams of a meadow she has never visited — “high, between two ridges, where the grass was the colour of old gold and a woman was weaving.” Book Two, Day 7 · Sarahan, 1,920 m
A dream of a woman weaving in a golden meadow “You dreamed of me,” the Shepherd told her.
v
The third evening, after reading the Shepherd's pages —
All three
The Shepherd's grandmother taught her the natti pattern — “the one that follows the river.” The original weaver walked the Tirthan from its source to the junction with the Sainj and translated the walk into thread. The original weaver's name is the same name as the muleteer's grandmother. Book Three, early autumn · above Khanag, 2,800 m
The grandmother is one person remembered in two traditions The muleteer remembers her song. The Shepherd remembers her weaving. Different media, same source.
vi
After closing the last notebook —
The ecology
Three routes. One landscape. The Trader sees the passes. The Pilgrim sees the valleys. The Shepherd sees the meadows between them. Together they describe a single watershed — but none of them sees the whole.

I am the first person to hold all three at once.
