* The Outer Frame: Jalori

** Prefatory Note on Route-Books

In the bazaars of Kullu and Rampur, a small notebook costs less
than a cup of chai. The cover is soft card, the pages ruled in
blue, the binding stitched loosely enough that the notebook can
lie flat when opened on a stone or a knee. They are sold for
schoolwork, for accounts, for letters never sent. But on the
mountain roads — the mule tracks over Jalori and Bashleo, the
pilgrim trails to Sarahan and Chitkul, the unmarked paths that
shepherds follow between summer and winter pastures — these
notebooks become route-books: daily records of distance, weather,
encounters, and the small observations that travellers make when
they are alone with a landscape and a pen.

A route-book is not a diary. A diary records what the writer feels.
A route-book records what the road offers: the gradient, the
weather, the state of the bridges, the price of dal at the last
dhaba, the bird that appeared on the stone wall at the turn
above the gorge. A diary faces inward. A route-book faces the
trail.

Most route-books travel with their owners. But some do not. A
traveller who crosses a pass may leave her notebook at the serai
on the other side — the pages full, the journey continuing, the
book too heavy to carry for sentiment when every gram matters on
the high road. Or a traveller may simply forget. Or — and this
the keeper of the serai at Jalori told the Thread Walker with
the matter-of-factness of a woman who has been keeping a serai
for forty years — a traveller may leave a route-book deliberately,
the way a person leaves a stone on a cairn: not because she needs
to, but because leaving something behind at the highest point is
what the crossing asks of you.

What follows is from three route-books found in a serai near the
top of Jalori Pass, and from the Thread Walker's notebooks,
written during three evenings in late autumn — the last evenings
before the snow closed the pass for the season.


** I. The Serai at Jalori

The Thread Walker arrived at the serai in the afternoon, climbing
the last switchbacks through forest that changed with every hundred
metres of altitude — oak giving way to spruce, spruce giving way
to birch, the birch thinning until the trees stood apart from one
another like people who have been walking together and are beginning
to drift, each finding her own pace, her own line through the
rocks and the low scrub that replaces forest at the altitude where
winter arrives first and leaves last.

The serai was stone and timber. Not Kath-Kuni — too high for the
elaborate alternating courses of the lower valleys — but the
simpler construction of the passes: dry-stone walls a metre thick,
a timber frame for the roof, slate laid in overlapping courses
the way a bird lays its feathers, each one covering the gap below.
A single window on the south side, the glass thick and
greenish, the kind of glass that does not offer a view but
offers light, which at this altitude is more valuable. A hearth
in the centre of the main room, the smoke finding its way through
a hole in the roof that was not quite a chimney and not quite an
accident but something between the two — an arrangement that
worked, in the way that arrangements at high altitude work: not
by design but by the accumulation of winters that teach the
building what it needs.

The keeper was old. The Thread Walker could not guess her age —
at this altitude, at this latitude, age is a function of weather
as much as years, and a woman who has spent forty winters at a
pass looks older than her years the way a deodar that grows at
the treeline looks older than its rings: the trunk twisted by
the prevailing wind, the bark furrowed by frost, the shape not
of growth but of endurance.

She greeted the Thread Walker without surprise. In the way of
keepers, she had known someone was climbing — the sound of boots
on the stone path carried further than the boots themselves,
and at this altitude, where the air is thin enough that sound
travels differently, a keeper learns to read the approach the
way a fisherman reads the river: from the rhythm, the pace,
the weight.

/You walk alone/, the keeper said. /And you carry a notebook./

/I carry two/, the Thread Walker said. /One for what I see.
One for what I think about what I see./

The keeper considered this. /I do not read/, she said. /But I
keep notebooks that others have left. If you want to read,
I have three that may interest you./

She went to a shelf — the same kind of shelf the Thread Walker
had seen in serais throughout the valleys, a plank of deodar
darkened by smoke and age, set into the wall above the hearth
where the warmth would keep whatever sat on it dry through the
monsoon and the snowmelt — and brought down a wooden box. Not
large. The size of a man's two hands placed side by side.

Inside the box were notebooks. Dozens of them — thin, bazaar-bought,
their covers stained by weather and altitude and the particular
marks of different valleys: red dust from the Sutlej side, grey
slate-dust from the Seraj hills, the faint green of lichen from
the high pastures.

/Travellers leave them/, the keeper said. /At the top of the pass,
or here at the serai. I do not read them. But I sort them./

/How do you sort them if you cannot read?/

The keeper held up a notebook — its cover stained the deep red
of laterite, the corners worn smooth, a faint smell of woodsmoke
and something else, something the Thread Walker recognised as the
smell of pack animals, the particular musk of a mule train.

/This one came from the Kullu side/, the keeper said. /Heading
to Spiti. I can tell from the red dust — that is Mandi road
before Aut. And the mule smell — no one takes mules to Kinnaur
any more, only to Spiti, where the road is still a mule road
above Kunzum./

She held up another — thinner, the cover not stained but warped,
as though it had been carried close to the body in rain.

/This one came from the Seraj side. Heading down to Rampur and
then east — to Kinnaur, Sarahan, perhaps Chitkul. The warping
is from the monsoon. A trader would have kept it in a pack,
wrapped. This one was carried in a breast pocket. Close to the
body. A pilgrim./

And a third — not a bazaar notebook but a sheaf of loose pages,
some lined, some blank, held together by a piece of twine. The
pages were small, torn from larger sheets. Some had writing.
Some had drawings — small, precise sketches of plants, of rock
faces, of the pattern of water over stones.

/This one never crossed the pass/, the keeper said. /She came
and went on this side. The pages are dry — no monsoon warping,
no mule dust. And the drawings — only someone who stays draws
like this. A traveller sketches what she sees once. This person
sketched the same stream three times, in three seasons. She
lives here./

The Thread Walker looked at the three notebooks. /These are
from the same year?/

/The same autumn. The Trader came first, in early September,
heading to Spiti before the snow. The Pilgrim came two weeks
later, heading to Kinnaur for the Dussehra at Sarahan. The
Shepherd — her pages appear all year, a few at a time. She
brings them when she comes down for salt./

/Did they meet?/

/Not here. The Trader was gone before the Pilgrim arrived. The
Shepherd does not come to the serai — she leaves her pages on
the stone outside, under a rock, the way she would leave an
offering at a shrine. I find them. I put them in the box./

She placed the three notebooks on the flat stone beside the
hearth, where the firelight was steadiest.

/Read them/, she said. /You are the first person to hold all
three at once./


* Route-Book One: The Trader

** From the first route-book:

/The Thread Walker opened the Trader's notebook after the
evening meal — roti and dal and a tea so strong it stained the
aluminium cup. The keeper had banked the fire for the night.
The lantern cast a circle of light on the stone floor that
included the notebook and the Thread Walker's hands and nothing
else. She began to read./


*** Day 1. Kullu to Naggar.

Load: 4 sacks rock salt, Mandi bazaar rate, 14 rs/kg. 2 bundles
Kullu shawl — the fine weave, not the tourist grade. 1 bundle
dried apricot from the Sainj orchards. Total weight with pack
frame: 38 kg on the lead mule, 32 on the second.

Road: paved to Naggar castle, then mule track above the town.
The track follows the east bank of the Beas, climbing slowly.
Apple orchards on both sides — still in leaf, the last green
before the colour turns. Below, the river runs milky blue,
the glacier-melt still showing this late in the season.

Weather: clear dawn, frost on the grass at the river crossing.
Warm by ten. The mules are easy.

I bought rope at the shop below Naggar castle. The shopkeeper
said the pass is open but the north side has snow. "Go now," he
said. "In two weeks you will not go."

He also said a pilgrim had passed through three days ago, heading
for the Dussehra festival at Sarahan, taking the Jalori route.
I asked which way. He said: over the pass and down through Shoja
to Ani, then the long road to Rampur and east to the Sutlej. A
pilgrim's route. I go the other way — north, over Rohtang.


*** Day 2. Naggar to above Manali.

The road above Naggar narrows. Orchards give way to forest —
deodar first, then blue pine, the transition visible on the
hillside like a line drawn by altitude: below the line, the
cultivated trees, tended and pruned; above, the wild ones,
untended and — I think — more beautiful for it, in the way
that a river is more beautiful than a canal, not because it
is better but because it is not trying.

Crossed a bridge below Manali. The town is loud — tourists,
buses, the construction that has not stopped since they widened
the road to Rohtang. I did not stop. The mules do not like
Manali. The second mule balked at a truck and I lost twenty
minutes calming her.

Above Manali the valley narrows sharply. The Beas is louder
here, compressed between walls of dark rock. The gradient
steepens. By afternoon we had climbed 600 metres and the air
had changed — thinner, drier, the kind of air that makes the
throat ache and the lungs ask for more.

Camped at a flat place above the treeline. No fire — above the
treeline there is nothing to burn except the scrub, and the
scrub is needed by the marmots and the birds, and I do not
take from those who have less than I do. Cold rice from the
morning, wrapped in a cloth. The mules are nervous. They smell
snow.


*** Day 3. Marhi, and the muleteer's story.

Met a group of Lahauli traders coming down from Rohtang. They
travel light — no mules, just backs and rope. Their loads are
wool and dried yak cheese, heading to Manali for sale before
the pass closes. They say the snow on the north face is thick
above 3,800 metres but the track is still passable. "Only
just," one of them said. He had the hands of a man who works
with rope in cold — the fingers swollen at the joints, the
skin cracked and dark, the nails worn short by friction.

One of them — an older man, Ratan by name — walked with me for
an hour above Marhi. He was heading down, I was heading up, but
the trail is wide enough here that two can walk together without
one yielding to the other, and he slowed his pace to match mine,
the way an older walker will accommodate a younger one, not from
kindness exactly but from the pleasure of company on a trail
where company is rare.

Ratan told me about his grandmother, who had crossed Rohtang
seventy times. "She could read the weather from the lichen on
the stones near the top," he said. "Green means the pass is open.
Yellow means wait a day. Red means turn back — the wind will come
before you reach the other side."

I asked how lichen could predict weather. He laughed — not the
laugh of a man dismissing a question but the laugh of a man who
has asked the same question and received an answer that was not
an answer.

"She said the lichen knows the stone, and the stone knows the
wind, and the wind knows the snow. She said: do not ask the
sky. Ask the stone. The stone has been here longer."

His grandmother, he said, had composed a song about the crossing.
He did not sing it — the wind above Marhi was too strong for
singing, and besides, he said, the song was not for the open
trail but for the fire, for the evening, for the time when the
crossing is behind you and the memory of it is better than the
thing itself.

But he told me the words of the last verse, because they named
a place:

/Above the snowline, below the summit,
where the meltwater runs two ways —
north to the river that goes to Lahaul,
south to the river that stays —
there is a meadow where the two waters
almost meet, and the grass is gold,
and the woman who lives there knows both rivers
the way a mother knows her children:
by the sound they make when they are cold./

I wrote this down. I do not know the meadow. I do not know the
woman. But the words "where the two waters almost meet" have
the precision of a place name, and I have learned — on this
road and on others — that the best directions come from songs,
because songs are carried by memory and memory does not survive
imprecision.


*** Day 5. Rohtang.

The crossing. 3,978 metres. Wind from the north — steady, not
gusting, the kind of wind that does not surprise you but does
not stop, and after two hours in it you cannot remember what
stillness feels like.

The mules crossed well. The second one — the nervous one —
was calm today, as though the altitude had settled her the way
it settles the air: by thinning it, by removing the noise
that lower altitudes carry, until what remains is only the
essential: breath, step, stone.

Snow on the north face. Below us, Lahaul opened — brown and
vast and dry, the opposite of the green Kullu we had left. The
Chandra river visible as a white thread in the valley floor.
Beyond it, the mountains of Spiti, which are not the Himalaya
of postcard and calendar — snow-bright, cloud-heavy — but the
Himalaya of geology: brown, folded, ancient, the rock exposed
without the courtesy of forest or meadow to soften it. A
landscape that does not try to be beautiful and is therefore
more honest than beautiful things often are.

At the top of the pass, I left a stone on the cairn. The
mules rested. I looked south — back at Kullu, which was
already a memory, a green deepening to blue in the distance,
the Beas invisible but present, the way a river is always
present in its valley even when you cannot see it: by the
shape of the land, by the direction of the trees, by the
quality of the light, which in a river valley is never the
light of a ridge but always the light of a channel, gathered
and reflected by water.


*** Day 6. Descent to Lahaul.

The north side of Rohtang is a different country. The green
is gone. The air is dry. The rivers here — the Chandra, the
Bhaga — run grey, not blue; the glacier-melt carries a
different silt, a different geology, and the water that was
turquoise on the Kullu side is the colour of slate on this
side, as though the pass were not just a geographical
divide but a boundary between two kinds of light.

At Khoksar I bought tea and a chapatti. The shopkeeper was
Lahauli — his face darker than the Kullu faces, the features
sharper, the eyes narrowed by decades of high-altitude sun
and wind. He spoke Hindi with an accent that placed the
emphasis differently, the rhythm of a language that has
travelled through Tibetan country and picked up the cadence
of that drier, higher speech.

"The Kunzum road is open," he said. "Batal is three days. Four
with mules."

At Batal I will decide: press on to Kunzum or wait for the
weather. The rock salt will sell well in Spiti — they need it
for the winter, for the animals, for the preservation of meat
that must last from October to May. The shawls I am less
certain of. Spiti wears wool, not shawl. But the Spiti
monasteries buy Kullu shawl for offerings — draped over
stupas, given to lamas, sent as gifts to allied monasteries
in Ladakh. The apricots will sell anywhere.


*** Day 9. Kunzum.

The pass. 4,550 metres. Higher than Rohtang, but somehow
gentler — the approach is gradual, the road winding through a
valley that narrows without steepening, so that the altitude
arrives like a slow tide, not a cliff.

At the top, a stone enclosure — not a shrine exactly, but a
place where travellers stop and leave things: coins, ribbons,
packets of incense, and a flag of prayer flags that has been
there so long the fabric has become part of the stones,
the colours faded to a grey that is neither cloth nor rock
but the colour of something that has been exposed to altitude
until it has become altitude.

Inside the enclosure, on the largest stone, a carved mark. I
do not know this mark. It is not Tibetan script, not Devanagari,
not any writing I have seen on this road or the roads before it.
A figure — vaguely human, or perhaps an animal standing upright,
the lines worn by weather and the hands of travellers who have
touched it the way people touch carvings, as though the touching
were itself a kind of reading, a way of knowing a figure that
does not require knowing what it means.

I sketched it in the margin of the page. Perhaps someone in
Spiti will know.

Below us, Spiti opened. Brown, vast, dry as a bone. The first
village — Losar — visible as a cluster of white-walled houses
at the junction of two streams. The mules quickened. They smelled
habitation.

The rock salt will sell.

/The Trader's voice is her prices and her distances. She sees
the world in quantities — the weight of the mules' loads, the
rate of salt, the number of days. But between the quantities,
she sees what the road offers without being asked: the mules'
nervousness, the quality of the light on two sides of a pass,
the muleteer's grandmother's song, the carved mark at Kunzum
that she does not recognise but sketches anyway./

/She records a song she has never heard sung, from a man she
met for an hour on a trail above Marhi. The song names a meadow
"where the two waters almost meet." She writes it down because
the words have "the precision of a place name." She does not
know where the meadow is. She does not know that someone lives
there./

/There is a mark at Kunzum. She has never seen it before. She
sketches it. I have seen this mark. I have seen it at Nirmand,
at Sarahan, at the wooden temples where the stone-cutters work
— the same figure, worn by the same weather, touched by the
same hands. But the Trader has not walked those valleys. She
has walked the high road, the trade road, the road of passes
and mules and salt. The mark at Kunzum is the same tradition
carried to a different altitude./

/I will read the Pilgrim's book tomorrow. I wonder whether she
has seen this mark./
