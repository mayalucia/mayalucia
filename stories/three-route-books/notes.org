* Origin

Conversation about Jan Potocki's /Manuscrit trouvé à Saragosse/
(1797–1815) and Wojciech Has's 1965 film adaptation. The nested
narrative structure — characters become narrators, disconnected tales
resolve into hidden genealogy — maps onto MāyāLucIA's architecture:
found manuscript = sūtra relay, substrates-become-observers =
characters-become-narrators, WP interconnection = Gomelez genealogy.

The novella is the first long-form piece in the project. All previous
stories are standalone short pieces (2,000–5,000 words). This aims
for 15,000–25,000 words across three route-books plus a frame
narrative.

* Setting

** The Serai at Jalori

Jalori Pass, 3,120 m, between Kullu and the Outer Seraj. The pass
connects the Beas watershed (north) to the Sutlej watershed (south)
via the Seraj hills. A serai at the pass — real or plausible — where
travellers stop before descending into whichever valley they are
heading toward.

The serai keeper is not the same keeper as in "The Serai's Register"
(story #13, set at the Tirthan–Sainj junction below Larji). That
keeper sorted tiles by identity. This keeper sorts route-books by
route. Different serai, different altitude, different practice.

** The Three Routes

- *Route 1 — Kullu to Spiti via Rohtang/Kunzum*: The Trader's route.
  North, then east, over two high passes. Salt, wool, dried apricots.
  A journey of weeks.

- *Route 2 — Jalori to Kinnaur via Sutlej gorge*: The Pilgrim's
  route. East and south, descending to the Sutlej, then upstream
  through Rampur, Sarahan, Kalpa. Shrines, wooden temples, the
  Buddhist–Hindu transition.

- *Route 3 — The Seraj high pastures*: The Shepherd's route. Neither
  pass. The meadows between the Beas and Sutlej watersheds, where the
  two river systems nearly touch. Seasonal, circular, unmarked on
  trade maps.

** Geography Constraint

Real places only. The human has walked some of this landscape. Every
named village, river, pass, and meadow must be real and correctly
placed. Geography is the vessel, not decoration.

* Structure

** Outer Frame

The Thread Walker arrives at the serai at Jalori. The keeper shows
her a box of route-books — small notebooks, the kind sold in bazaars
in Kullu or Rampur, that travellers carry and sometimes leave behind.
Three of them, left by different travellers in different seasons,
catch the Thread Walker's attention because they seem to describe the
same landscape from three altitudes without knowing the others exist.

The Thread Walker reads them over three evenings. The outer frame is
her reading and her notebook entries.

** The Nesting (Saragossa technique)

Each route-book contains:
- Level 0: The traveller's daily observations (the route-book itself)
- Level 1: Stories told to the traveller by people she meets on the
  road (a muleteer, a stone-cutter, a herder)
- Level 2: Stories /within/ those stories — the muleteer's
  grandmother's song, the stone-cutter's teacher's technique, the
  herder's ancestor's migration

The Saragossa mechanism: a character introduced at level 1 in one
route-book turns out to be a character at level 1 in another. The
muleteer in the Trader's route-book is the stone-cutter's brother in
the Pilgrim's. The shrine in the Pilgrim's account is the Shepherd's
winter camp. The song the muleteer's grandmother sang was composed by
a weaver whose loom the Shepherd inherited.

The reader discovers the connections. None of the three travellers
do. The Thread Walker begins to see them on the second evening.

** Resolution

Not a conspiracy (as in Potocki) but an ecology: the three routes
are one landscape seen from three altitudes. The trader sees the
passes. The pilgrim sees the valleys. The shepherd sees the meadows
between them. Together they describe a single system — watershed,
trade, devotion — that none of them sees whole.

This maps to the substrate diversity finding: no single lineage sees
the whole architecture. The union of diverse perspectives reveals
the ecology.

* Document Type

Found document: three route-books, read by the Thread Walker at a
serai. Frame: Thread Walker's notebook. Inner: the route-books
themselves, with nested oral accounts.

* Voice

The route-books have three different voices:
- The Trader writes in short, factual sentences. Ledger voice.
  Quantities, distances, weather.
- The Pilgrim writes in flowing, devotional prose. Shrine descriptions,
  dreams, vows.
- The Shepherd writes in seasonal, observational fragments. Grass,
  weather, animal health. Few complete sentences. Lists.

The Thread Walker's frame voice is the same as all other stories.

* Relation to Existing Stories

- *The Serai's Register* (#13): Different serai, same concept of
  travellers converging. The register keeper sorts by identity; this
  keeper sorts by route. Companion, not sequel.
- *The Weaver's Loom* (#11): The loom at Nahin. The Shepherd may have
  inherited this loom or one like it.
- *The Three Readers* (#15): Three readers, different traditions,
  same rock. Structural parallel — but that was a short story. This
  is the long form.
- *The Three Scribes' Brief* (#20): Same trio structure but about
  building (authoring), not reading. This novella subsumes both
  reading and building.

* Open Questions

- How many levels of nesting can prose sustain before the reader loses
  the thread? Potocki managed 8 in 600+ pages. In 20,000 words, 3
  levels may be the maximum.
- Should the route-books be presented sequentially (Book 1, Book 2,
  Book 3) or interleaved (Day 1 of each, Day 2 of each)?
  Sequential is simpler and lets the reader discover connections
  across books. Interleaved is more Saragossa-like but harder to
  sustain.
- The comedy question: Potocki is funny. Can the route-books have
  humour without breaking the MāyāLucIA register? The Trader's
  matter-of-fact observations of absurdity might be the vehicle.
- Illustration: the existing pipeline produces matplotlib figures.
  For a novella, we may want a different approach — maps, route
  diagrams, botanical/geological sketches in the margin style.
