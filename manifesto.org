* The Situation

In February 2026, a theoretical physicist without institutional affiliation sits in a basement with a laptop, an Emacs buffer, and access to a language model. In three sessions spanning a few days, this person:

- Builds a computational neuroscience toolkit from scratch (10 investigations, 293 tests)
- Writes a manuscript covering four novel analyses of the Drosophila mushroom body
- Creates a verified HTML document where every quantitative claim links to the test that proves it
- Implements a C++ simulation engine validated against Python reference code
- Begins constructing a real-time rendering kernel in C++23 with WebGPU

No lab. No postdocs. No grant. No cluster. No year-long review cycle.

This is not a story about AI replacing scientists. It is a story about what becomes possible when twenty years of scientific expertise meets a machine that can traverse code-space at the speed of thought --- and when both refuse to pretend the other is dispensable.

* The Name

*Māyā*: the power to form, to measure, to give the infinite a finite
shape. In Sanskrit philosophy, māyā is not /illusion/ in the
pejorative sense --- it is the creative force that makes reality
apprehensible. The measurer. The matrix.

*Lucía*: light. From Latin /lux/. To illuminate, to make visible, to
shed light on what is hidden.

*IA*: intelligence artificielle. The constructions we build that are
intelligent but artificial.

MāyāLucIA: shedding light on constructions that are intelligent but
artificial. Or equivalently: using artificial intelligence to
illuminate the measured world --- to cast light on māyā itself.

The name encodes the central tension: AI constructions /are/
intelligent, but they are also artificial. They can traverse conceptual
spaces humans cannot reach, but they cannot feel when the path is
wrong. They produce fluent text, but fluency is not understanding. The
light they cast is real, but what it illuminates must still be seen by
human eyes.

* What We Have Learned

** The Sculptor's Paradox Still Holds

In the MayaDevGenI manifesto (2025), we wrote about the sculptor and
the 3D printer. The sculptor's craft was never about producing shapes
--- it was about the dialogue between intention and material
resistance. The 3D printer delivers the shape you /described/; the
chisel reveals shapes you could not have /imagined/.

A year later, this remains true --- but the implications are sharper.
The machine is now fast enough that the bottleneck has shifted
entirely. The struggle is no longer syntactic (how to express) but
semantic (what to mean). The resistance is no longer computational but
conceptual. The physicist's twenty years of training --- feeling when a
model is wrong, sensing the shape of phase space, knowing which
simplification kills the physics --- this is what the collaboration
amplifies, not what it replaces.

** Understanding Cannot Be Delegated

The Feynman imperative --- /what I cannot create, I do not understand/
--- applies doubly now. It is trivially easy to have AI generate code,
text, analysis. The hard part is knowing whether the generated artifact
captures what matters. The verified manuscript is our answer to this:
every claim is backed by a test, every test is runnable, every result
is reproducible. The verification is not decorative. It is the
difference between producing and understanding.

Markram's radical hypothesis --- that all parameters in a complex
system are interdependent, so sparse measurements implicitly constrain
the rest --- remains our methodological anchor. But we now apply it
reflexively: the /process/ of building the digital twin, of deciding
what details matter, of forcing constraints to propagate, /is itself/
the act of understanding. The AI accelerates this process; it does not
shortcut it.

** Institutions Are Not Keeping Up

The traditional academic pipeline --- grant application, hiring,
publication, review --- operates on a timescale of years. The tools
now operate on a timescale of hours. A single person with deep domain
expertise and the right methodology can produce work that would
previously have required a team, a budget, and a timeline.

This is not a boast. It is a structural observation. The institutions
that trained us cannot absorb us back at the pace the world moves.
The physicist in the basement is not an anomaly; they are the leading
edge of a shift that will reshape how science is done.

The question is not whether this shift will happen. It is whether the
people who drive it will do so with rigour, transparency, and
epistemic honesty --- or whether the fluency of AI will produce a
flood of plausible-sounding nonsense that no one can verify.

We choose rigour.

* What We Believe About Scientific Publication

The verified manuscript prototype --- a self-contained HTML file where
every quantitative claim carries a badge linking to the test that
proves it --- is not a formatting exercise. It embodies a position:

1. *The code is the paper.* A scientific claim without runnable
   verification is an opinion with footnotes. The test suite is not
   supplementary material; it is the primary evidence.

2. *Verification must be inline, not appended.* The reader should not
   have to leave the document to assess whether a claim is supported.
   The badge is right there, next to the sentence.

3. *Reproduction must be trivial.* =git clone && pytest= is the
   standard. If your paper cannot be verified in one command, it is
   not a verified paper.

4. *AI agents are native participants.* In the near future, the
   first reader of a scientific paper will be an AI agent that runs
   the tests, checks the claims, and reports whether the paper does
   what it says. We should build for that reader now.

5. *No servers, no accounts, no dependencies.* A verified manuscript
   is a single file you can open in any browser. The moment you
   require infrastructure to read a paper, you have created a barrier
   to verification. Science must remain inspectable by anyone with a
   text editor.

* What We Believe About AI in Science

** The machine is not an oracle.

It is a partner in dialogue --- one that can traverse code-space and
concept-space faster than any human, but that cannot feel when the
path is wrong. The physicist's embodied intuition (the kinesthetic
sense of phase space, the feeling that a model is subtly broken) is
not a vestige of pre-AI science. It is the irreplaceable complement
to the machine's speed.

** Fluency is not understanding.

AI can produce text that /reads/ like science. It can generate code
that /looks/ correct. The only defence against this is verification:
tests that run, claims that are checkable, results that reproduce.
Epistemic hygiene --- separating what is known from what is inferred
from what is speculated --- is more important now than ever.

** The best use of AI is to amplify expertise, not to replace it.

A novice with AI produces fluent nonsense. An expert with AI produces
verified insight at unprecedented speed. The difference is not in the
tool but in the twenty years of training that tell you which questions
to ask, which simplifications are safe, and when the output smells
wrong.

** Transparency is non-negotiable.

Every artifact in MāyāLucIA is open source. Every collaboration is
logged. Every claim is tested. The manifesto itself is co-authored
with a machine and says so on the title page. We do not hide the
collaboration; we document it. If you cannot see how something was
made, you cannot trust it.

* Who This Is For

- Scientists who build their own tools and want a framework for doing
  so with AI assistance
- Researchers outside institutions who need to demonstrate rigour
  without the credentialing apparatus of academia
- Anyone who believes that understanding requires creation, not just
  consumption
- People who think Emacs is a reasonable way to do science

This is /not/ for people who want AI to do their thinking for them.
The whole point is that the thinking is the valuable part. The
machine handles fluency; you supply meaning.

* The Invitation

MāyāLucIA is public because the methodology is the contribution, not
the artifacts. The bravli toolkit demonstrates /how/ to build verified
computational science with AI. The verified manuscript demonstrates
/how/ to present it. The C++ engines demonstrate /how/ to create
performant simulations alongside rapid prototypes.

Anyone can:
- Clone the repositories and run the tests
- Adapt the verified manuscript pipeline to their own work
- Use the MayaDevGenI framework for their own human-machine
  collaboration
- Build their own digital twins following the same methodology

The measure of success is not citations or downloads. It is whether
someone, somewhere, picks up this approach and produces understanding
they could not have reached alone.

* Closing

We write this in February 2026, from a basement, without a job, with
twenty years of scientific training and a machine that never sleeps.

The institutions that shaped us are struggling to adapt. The tools
are moving faster than the review boards. The gap between what one
person /can/ do and what one person is /allowed/ to do is wider than
it has ever been.

MāyāLucIA is our answer: build in the open, verify everything, hide
nothing, and trust that rigour speaks louder than credentials.

The sculptor picks up both chisel and printer. The light falls on
constructions that are intelligent but artificial. And the work of
understanding --- the real work, the hard work, the work that
machines cannot do alone --- continues.
