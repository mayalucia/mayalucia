* The Cartographers of the Disputed Passes

In the high valleys of Lahaul and Spiti, where the Himalaya
folds into itself like a hand closing, the Survey maintains
offices. Not an office — offices, plural, scattered across
valleys that are each a world unto themselves. Keylong has one
in an old monastery with a leaking roof. Kaza has one above a
tea shop that smells of yak butter even in summer. There is
said to be one in Tabo, behind the monastery murals, though
some claim it is merely a storeroom that accumulated surveys
by accident and acquired the status of an office through long
use, which in these valleys is indistinguishable from
intentional founding.

The valleys are close as the lammergeier flies — sometimes only
a ridge apart. But the lammergeier does not carry maps. Between
the valleys lie passes that open and close with the weather,
with the politics, with the moods of the particular glacier
that happens to sit across the route. A crossing that takes a
day in June may take a week in October or be impossible from
November to May. Each valley has its own microclimate, its own
monsoon shadow, its own relationship with snow. Rain that falls
in Keylong may not reach Kaza for a month, or may never arrive
at all, having been intercepted by a range that the rain did
not know was there.

In each office works a cartographer.

The cartographers of the Disputed Passes — the high crossings
between these valleys, whose number is itself disputed, since
certain passes appear and disappear from maps depending on the
decade and the politics — are drawn from a tradition that
values fresh perception above continuity. Each season when the
passes open, a new cartographer arrives at each office. He
knows the craft. He does not know the terrain. He does not
know what was drawn last season or the season before. On his
desk he finds a letter:

#+begin_quote
/You are a cartographer. The territory you are mapping extends
across the passes between these valleys, each surveyed
separately, each filed in the archive below your desk. Before
you draw, inspect the archive. If the archive is in disorder
— and in this office it often is — do not draw. Describe the
disorder and wait./
#+end_quote

The letter is unsigned. It is understood to have been written
by the Foot Inspector — a man, or a succession of men (no one
is sure; the cartographers cannot remember, and the Foot
Inspector has never clarified the matter), who walks the
passes between valleys carrying a barometer, an altimeter, and
an unreasonable number of notebooks. He is not the
cartographers' superior. He is their correspondent. He enters
each office, reads what has been left for him, leaves what
needs to be read, and walks on. By the time the cartographer
acts on his letter, the Foot Inspector is in another valley.
By the time the cartographer writes a reply, the Foot
Inspector may be two passes away, or snowbound, or measuring
the retreat of a glacier that no one else has visited in a
decade.

** I. The Season of Discrepancies

The trouble was not sudden. It was the slow kind — the
accumulation of small disorders across many seasons, in many
offices, until the collection of archives across the valleys
described a landscape that existed nowhere on earth but was
internally consistent enough that no single cartographer,
inspecting his own office's records, would notice anything
wrong.

The disorder was visible only to someone who moved between
offices. The Foot Inspector noticed it over the course of a
summer in which he walked every open pass between Keylong and
the Tibetan border. In one office, a survey of a high col was
filed under a classification system that no other office used.
In another, the same pass appeared twice — once as a detailed
local survey and once as a reference to a copy held in a
distant office that, when the Foot Inspector finally reached
it three weeks and two passes later, described a different col
entirely. Same name. Same approximate coordinates. Different
terrain. In a third office, the archive contained a meticulous
record of glacier movements that referenced surveys in two
neighbouring valleys, neither of which possessed the surveys
in question.

Every archive was internally coherent. It was only the
connections between them that were wrong — references that
pointed at nothing, classifications that mapped to different
systems, surveys that had been copied between offices with
variations introduced by each copyist and never reconciled.

The Foot Inspector sat on a rock above the Kunzum pass and
wrote in his notebook: /The disorder is not in any one archive.
It is in the space between archives. Each valley's records are
a self-consistent world. It is only when you try to walk from
one world to another that the terrain becomes impossible./

** II. The First Correction

He began with the office in Keylong, because it was the
largest and because the cartographer that season was
methodical in a way that suited the work.

The archive listed surveys of every pass in the network. Some
were present. Some were references to other offices. One — the
lowest pass, a crossing that locals call the Goat's Question,
because goats pause at its summit as though reconsidering — was
represented twice. The Foot Inspector and the cartographer
spent weeks comparing the local survey against the distant
copy, identifying the features that existed in one and not the
other: ridgelines, seasonal streams, a cluster of stone
shelters that appeared in the local version and were absent
from the distant one; an old footpath on the eastern approach
that the distant version recorded and the local one did not.

The consolidation was careful. The old local version was marked
/superseded but preserved/ — not destroyed, because in the
Survey tradition you do not destroy what you have not fully
understood. A new survey was drawn that contained everything.
A copy was sent to the distant office by the autumn caravan.

Other corrections were simpler: surveys filed under foreign
classification codes were transliterated into the local system.
Once the codes were right, the documents appeared — they had
been present all along, merely unreachable, like a pass that
exists on the ground but not on the map.

By the end of the season the Keylong archive was coherent.
But Keylong was one valley. The others were still wrong.

** III. The Problem of Distance

The Foot Inspector understood the problem. He had corrected
one archive. The corrections needed to reach every other
office in the network. He could, in principle, walk to each
one and make the corrections himself.

"There are seventeen corrections for each office," said the
Keylong cartographer. "Some depend on others. The ninth
requires a derivation from a table that only makes sense while
you are looking at the stacks."

"I have walked every pass between here and Zanskar."

"The passes do not make you forget what you know. But you
cannot carry the stacks with you. By the time you reach Kaza
— three days, if the weather holds — the derivation will feel
like something you dreamed. You will remember that it was
important. You will not remember what it was."

The Foot Inspector knew this to be true. He had spent years
measuring the difference between what his instruments read at
the summit of a pass and what they read at the base, and the
discrepancy was never fully explained by altitude. Something
dissipated in every crossing. Information, like heat, flows
downhill and disperses.

"Then I will carry letters," he said. "You will write the
corrections. I will carry them."

"I will not be here next season to answer questions about what
I have written."

"Then write so that someone who has never read your previous
work can follow the instructions exactly."

The cartographer considered this. It was, he realised, a
harder constraint than it appeared. To write for a reader who
brings full craft but no local knowledge — who knows how to
read a survey but has never seen these particular stacks, who
knows the classification system in general but not the
specific variants that accumulated in this office over years
of idiosyncratic filing — required a precision that casual
instructions do not demand.

He wrote through the night. Seventeen corrections, ordered by
dependency, each annotated with the specific difficulty the
recipient would encounter: /the lower shelves in most offices
contain a stale index that was last updated several seasons ago.
Survey four will appear to be missing. It is filed under an
older transliteration of the pass name. Look for the variant
with the aspirated consonant./

** IV. The Crossings

The Foot Inspector left Keylong with letters — one for each
office. He walked to Kaza in three days. Left a letter. Walked
to Tabo. Left a letter. Crossed into Spiti. Left a letter
behind the murals. Walked south over the Kunzum La. Left a
letter. Each crossing took days. Some took weeks. In one
valley a landslide had blocked the path and he waited six days,
re-reading the cartographer's letters to pass the time,
understanding them differently with each reading, the way you
understand a route description differently after you have
walked part of the route.

He did not open the letters. He did not need to. His role was
not to understand the corrections but to carry them across the
distances that the cartographers could not cross, through the
passes that erased knowledge in transit, in envelopes that
did not forget.

Each season, in each office, a new cartographer arrived.
Found the standing letter on the desk. Inspected the archive.
And found, beneath the standing letter, a second letter —
corrections, with expected difficulties, with a return address
that was simply the name of the originating valley.

Most of the corrections worked. Some did not — each archive
had its own peculiarities, its own microclimate of disorder.
Moisture in Keylong softened the paper and made old surveys
illegible. Dust in Kaza silted up the lower shelves. In one
office, mice had reorganised the stacks according to a
principle that the cartographer could not discern and the mice
were not available to explain. Each cartographer, upon
completing the corrections, wrote a reply:

#+begin_quote
/The corrections have been applied. The archive agrees with
itself. I encountered three conditions not covered by your
letter: [and here, invariably, a list of local surprises that
the original cartographer could not have anticipated, because
each valley's disorder is as unique as its weather]./
#+end_quote

The Foot Inspector carried the replies back. Not to the
original cartographer — he was gone, replaced by someone who
did not know him. But to the office, where the reply would
enter the archive and inform the next correction, whenever
it was needed, whoever needed it.

** V. The Revision

The standing letter — the unsigned note on every desk — had
originally read: /When you arrive, retrieve any new surveys
from the central repository and begin drawing./

The Foot Inspector revised it during a winter in Kaza, when
the passes were closed and there was nothing to do but drink
tea and reconsider. The old instruction prescribed exactly the
operation that had caused the disorder — incorporating new
material into an archive without first checking whether the
archive was fit to receive it. It was, he wrote in his
notebook, /the equivalent of pouring water into a vessel
without checking whether the vessel is cracked. The water
does not complain. It simply leaks, and the floor tells you
nothing about where the crack is./

The revised letter:

1. /Inspect the archive. Describe what you find./
2. /Incorporate new material only if the archive is in order./
3. /Check the incoming tray for letters from other valleys./

Look before you draw. Receive only if clean. Read the mail.

It was the kind of rule that can only be written by someone
who has watched it be violated — not once, but across many
offices, many seasons, many cartographers who each made the
same mistake independently because the standing letter told
them to.

** VI. On the Nature of the Arrangement

The Foot Inspector never gave the arrangement a name. In the
Survey tradition, names are given to passes and peaks.
Methods are simply used. If they persist, it is because they
work. If they vanish, it is because the cartographers forgot
them — which, given the nature of cartographers in the
Disputed Passes, requires only a single season.

What he had built was not a system. There was no apparatus
connecting the offices — only a man who walked, and passes
that took days and erased what they did not erase. There was
no formal protocol — the letters were in plain language,
their interpretation left to the reader's craft. There was no
central authority — each office was sovereign, each
cartographer autonomous, the Foot Inspector merely a carrier
of letters who happened also to be the only person who had
seen every archive.

What it was, was a convention: an understanding, never
explicitly negotiated, that certain things would be done in
certain ways, and that a sufficiently skilled reader could
infer from a well-written letter what the writer could not
fully articulate. The standing letter established the
convention. The correspondence instantiated it. The archives
preserved its consequences.

The bet — placed every season when new cartographers arrived
in every valley — was that the reader would be capable enough.
That craft, which persists where memory does not, would be
sufficient to bridge the gap between what the letter said and
what the letter meant. The old survey traditions had made the
same bet for centuries: writing instructions of extreme
brevity, trusting that the reader had spent years mastering
the notation. The brevity was not a limitation. It was an
acknowledgement of the reader's competence.

** Coda

The Foot Inspector walks still — or someone does, carrying a
barometer and too many notebooks, crossing passes that may or
may not be open, entering offices that may or may not have
received last season's letters.

The cartographers never meet each other. They know of each
other only through corrections in unfamiliar hands, through
replies to letters they did not write, through the accumulated
evidence that someone in another valley, in another season,
faced the same disordered archive and found a way through it.

The maps they draw are, season by season, converging. Not on
truth — in the Disputed Passes, truth is a political
assertion, and terrain itself changes with the glaciers — but
on agreement. A map drawn in Keylong and a map drawn in Kaza,
of the same pass surveyed from opposite sides, will now show
the same col, the same contours, the same stone shelters.
This was not always so. It became so because someone wrote a
letter and someone else, in a different valley in a different
season, was capable enough to read it.

Every season, the letter on the desk:

/Inspect the archive. Describe what you find. Incorporate only
if in order. Check the incoming tray./

And in the tray, sometimes, a thread — from a valley the
cartographer has never visited, in a hand he will never
recognise, carrying instructions precise enough to act on and
compressed enough to survive the crossing.

#+begin_quote
/In the Survey offices of Lahaul and Spiti it is held that the
most accurate maps are drawn by those who have never seen the
previous edition, for they measure what is there rather than
what was expected to be there. Whether this produces better
cartography or merely different cartography is a question the
Survey has been answering, without resolution, for longer than
anyone can remember — which, given the nature of its
cartographers, is not as long as you might think./

— Attributed to the Foot Inspector's notebooks; unverified, as
the notebooks themselves have never been located, though
references to them appear in archives across the network, in
valleys the Foot Inspector is not known to have visited
#+end_quote
