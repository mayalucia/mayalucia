* What an Ecological Survey Is

An /ecological survey/ in this project is not a species list. It is a
stability analysis of a living system across deep time.

The survey template has three temporal layers, each requiring a
different kind of knowledge:

- *Construction* (Ma to ka) — how the ecological assemblage was built.
  Tectonic forces, paleoclimate, paleo-ecology, co-evolutionary
  pressure. This is the evidence for the attractor's depth: the deeper
  the basin, the more energy required to escape it.

- *Configuration* (decades to centuries) — the present state. Species,
  trophic webs, phenological calendars, nutrient cycles, human
  entanglement. This is the metastable configuration — what we observe,
  what we can still count and map.

- *Unravelling* (decades to centuries forward) — the transition dynamics.
  What happens when the perturbation exceeds the restoring forces.
  Glacier loss, treeline migration, phenological mismatch, trophic
  cascade, human displacement. Which transitions are gradual, which are
  threshold events, and which are irreversible.

Every species, every interaction, every seasonal rhythm occupies a
position in a stability landscape that is being tilted. The survey
must make this visible.

* Structure of a Survey

The template has five parts. Not every survey will need all five — a
survey of a well-studied region may have a thin Part A (deep time is
well-documented elsewhere) and a thick Part C (unravelling is the
urgent question). A first survey of a poorly-known region may stop at
Part B (configuration) with an explicit statement of what Part C
would need. But the skeleton is stable.

** Part A: Construction — Deep Time Ecology

Timescale: Ma to ka. The geological and evolutionary history that
assembled the present ecological configuration.

*** A.1 Tectonic and lithological setting

The rock and the forces that made it. Plate tectonics, suture zones,
metamorphic grade, uplift rates, erosion rates. The substrate —
literally — on which everything else stands. Include the tectonic
context that makes this region different from adjacent regions
(syntaxis, arc collision, rain shadow formation).

*** A.2 Paleoclimate

Monsoon history, rain shadow evolution, glacial cycles. How the
climate regime that shapes present ecology was itself shaped by
tectonic and orbital forcing. The key transitions:

- Monsoon onset and intensification (~25–8 Ma)
- Rain shadow deepening as mountains rise
- Pleistocene glacial cycles and their /regional/ expression
  (asynchrony with global ice volume in westerly-dominated ranges)
- Holocene optimum and subsequent neoglaciation
- Named aridification events (4.2 ka, 8.2 ka) and their regional
  signature

*** A.3 Paleo-ecology

What lived here when. Fossil evidence (or its absence and why).
Vegetation shifts inferred from pollen, isotope, and sediment records.
Megafauna presence and extinction. The C3/C4 transition. Treeline
history.

Be honest about evidentiary gaps — high-erosion environments destroy
fossils. The absence of evidence /is/ evidence of the erosional regime,
not evidence of absence.

*** A.4 The ecological attractor

The synthesis of A.1–A.3: what convergent configuration emerged, and
/why it was stable/. What feedback loops held it in place? What would
it take to break them?

This section is interpretive. It requires the surveyor to articulate
the stability mechanism — not just describe the state but explain the
basin. Examples:

- The monsoon–vegetation–erosion feedback (vegetation stabilises
  slopes → reduces erosion → allows soil development → supports more
  vegetation)
- The glacier–river–valley feedback (glacier melt feeds river →
  river incises valley → deeper valley traps cold air → sustains
  glacier)
- The predator–prey equilibrium (snow leopard limits ibex →
  ibex don't overgraze → alpine meadow persists → marmot habitat
  stable → golden eagle sustained)

The attractor is the starting point for Part C. You cannot assess
unravelling without knowing what is being unravelled.

** Part B: Configuration — The Present Ecological Web

Timescale: decades. The current state — what can be observed, counted,
and mapped.

*** B.1 Altitude zonation

The physical scaffold. A table of zones (elevation range, dominant
vegetation, key species, physical character) anchors the rest of
Part B. Every subsequent section references these zones.

Use the project's established format:

#+begin_example
| Zone | Elevation | Character | Key species |
|------+-----------+-----------+-------------|
| ...  | ...       | ...       | ...         |
#+end_example

*** B.2 Flora by zone

Dominant species, keystone species, endemic species, relict species.
For each species: physical description → ecology in the survey region
→ cultural significance → narrative interest → inline sources.

Include the decomposition layer: fungi (beyond just the economically
important ones), lichen colonisation, soil microbiology where known.
Lichen on young granite is relevant where uplift exposes new rock.

*** B.3 Fauna by zone

Mammals, birds, herpetofauna, invertebrates. Same per-entity format
as B.2. Invertebrates will be thin for most Himalayan regions — say
so explicitly rather than omitting the heading.

*** B.4 Trophic web

Who eats whom. The food chains that connect B.2 and B.3 into a web.
Decomposition chains (who breaks down whom after death). Pollination
networks (who pollinates what — high-altitude bumblebees, butterfly
assemblages, wind pollination).

This is the /plot/ that the species catalogue (B.2–B.3) lacks. The
species are actors; the trophic web is the story.

*** B.5 Phenological calendar

Seasonal rhythms as a table or timeline:

- Snowmelt timing by altitude
- Flowering periods (which species when)
- Breeding seasons (mammals, birds)
- Migration and transhumance
- Harvest calendars (morel, medicinal plants, crops)
- Monsoon onset/retreat (where applicable)
- Glacial melt peak

The phenological calendar is the first thing climate change disrupts.
Document it precisely so that Part C can describe the mismatch.

*** B.6 Aquatic ecology

Rivers, streams, glacial lakes, hot springs. Not just the water
itself but the life in it: snow trout (/Schizothorax/), mahseer,
macroinvertebrate communities. Hot springs as thermophilic niches.

Where the survey region's rivers are dammed or diverted for
hydropower, document the pre- and post-dam ecology.

*** B.7 Human entanglement

Agriculture, pastoralism, resource extraction, sacred groves, spirit
ecology. The human is not separate from the ecological web — the
human is a node. Grazing pressure, fire management, transhumance
routes, kuhl irrigation, timber extraction, morel harvesting,
medicinal plant collection, tourism.

Include the spirit traditions that are ecologically grounded: the
devta's sacred groves, the peri's alpine meadows, the gur's trance
triggered by seasonal cues, the bitan's juniper smoke. These are
/ecological relationships/ — they bind human behaviour to species
and seasons through ritual obligation.

*** B.8 Disturbance regime

Avalanche corridors, rockfall zones, flood pulses from glacial lake
outbursts (GLOFs), fire regimes. These are not disasters — they are
ecological events that reset succession.

What colonises after a GLOF? What grows in an avalanche corridor that
cannot grow in undisturbed forest? What species depend on disturbance?
The disturbance regime is part of the stability — removing it changes
the attractor.

** Part C: Unravelling — The Metastable State Under Perturbation

Timescale: decades to centuries forward. The transition dynamics.

*** C.1 The perturbation

What is changing. Regional temperature trends, precipitation regime
shifts, seasonality changes, extreme event frequency. Use regional
climate projections where available (IPCC AR6 WG1 regional data,
ICIMOD HKH Assessment, national climate reports). State the
uncertainty ranges — models disagree on precipitation more than on
temperature.

The perturbation is /not just warming/. It is a shift in the seasonal
structure — earlier snowmelt, later monsoon onset, more intense but
shorter precipitation events, increased interannual variability. The
system evolved for a rhythm; the rhythm is changing.

*** C.2 Glacier and cryosphere response

Mass balance trends, equilibrium line altitude (ELA) shifts, debris
cover dynamics. GLOF risk assessment (inventoried lakes, documented
events, projected risk).

The critical transition: from "glacial melt supplements river flow"
to "glacial melt /is/ river flow" (the point where rain and snowmelt
are insufficient). Peak water — the point of maximum meltwater before
declining glacier mass reduces flow — and when it is projected for
each major glacier system.

Where a regional anomaly exists (the Karakoram Anomaly — relative
glacial stability amid global retreat), document its mechanism and
assess whether it is ending.

*** C.3 Treeline and vegetation migration

Upslope advance: rate, species composition, spatial heterogeneity
(aspect, soil, grazing). The squeeze: invasive species from below
(Lantana, Ageratina) and advancing treeline from above — compressing
the zones between them.

What happens to the alpine meadow when the treeline rises through it?
What happens to the species that define the meadow (and the human
practices — pastoralism, morel harvesting — that depend on it)?

*** C.4 Faunal range shifts

Species tracking their climate envelope upward. Range compression:
when a species is already near the summit, there is nowhere higher
to go. Isolation: populations on separate peaks becoming genetically
disconnected as corridors warm.

Assess by functional group: apex predator (snow leopard), large
herbivore (ibex, markhor), keystone prey (marmot), scavenger
(lammergeier, griffon vulture), indicator species (western tragopan,
snow partridge).

*** C.5 Phenological mismatch

When seasonal cues decouple. Pollinators emerge before or after the
flowers they pollinate. Snowmelt timing shifts but breeding cues
(photoperiod) do not. Migratory species arrive to find the season
has moved.

Where spirit traditions are tied to phenological cues (apricot blossom
= time of peri selection, monsoon onset = devta procession season),
document the cultural mismatch alongside the ecological one. The
bitan's calendar and the chukar's calendar are both disrupted by the
same forcing.

*** C.6 Trophic cascade

What breaks when keystone links fail. If ibex populations decline
(range compression, disease, poaching compounded by climate stress),
what happens to the snow leopard? To the lammergeier that eats the
ibex bones the snow leopard left? To the alpine meadow the ibex no
longer grazes?

Trace at least one complete cascade from primary producer to apex
predator/terminal consumer. Include the decomposition pathway. Where
data is insufficient for quantitative modelling, describe the chain
qualitatively and flag the data gaps.

*** C.7 Human displacement and adaptation

Pastoralism collapse (transhumance routes blocked, alpine meadows
encroached). Hydropower vulnerability (glacial recession reducing
flow below design thresholds). Livelihood shifts (timber → tourism →
morels → ?). Cultural erosion (spirit traditions that depend on
ecological rhythms — if the apricot blooms early, does the bitan know?).

The compounding: when climate change, infrastructure development (dams,
roads), and demographic pressure act simultaneously. The dam displaces
the community whose oral tradition preserves the ecological knowledge
that would otherwise document the climate change. The knowledge is
destroyed by the same forces it could illuminate.

*** C.8 Transition dynamics

The synthesis of C.1–C.7. Which transitions are gradual and continuous?
Which are threshold events (tipping points)? Which are irreversible
on human timescales?

A table:

#+begin_example
| Transition | Type | Reversibility | Timescale | Certainty |
|------------+------+---------------+-----------+-----------|
| ...        | ...  | ...           | ...       | ...       |
#+end_example

Types: /gradual/ (linear response to forcing), /threshold/ (rapid
shift once a tipping point is crossed), /hysteretic/ (threshold event
that requires a different, stronger forcing to reverse).

This section is the survey's most interpretive and most valuable. It
is where the three Parts converge: the attractor (A) defines what was
stable, the configuration (B) defines what is at stake, and the
transition dynamics (C) assess what will be lost and what might be
gained.

** Part D: Cross-Reference and Synthesis

*** D.1 Parallel chains

Systematic alignment with other surveyed regions. Use an org table
with columns for each region. Align by: geology, altitude zonation,
dominant flora, dominant fauna, trophic structure, spirit tradition,
human practice, climate vulnerability.

*** D.2 The monsoon gradient as natural experiment

For Western Himalaya surveys: the gradient from full monsoon (Tirthan)
through the transition zone (Baspa, Sutlej) to the rain shadow (Spiti,
Diamer) is a natural experiment in what happens when you remove water
from the system. The same species assemblage — deodar → juniper,
terraced agriculture → none, devtas → peri — responds to the same
tectonic and orbital forcing under different precipitation regimes.

This gradient is educational: it shows what climate /already did/ to
ecology across space, which is a proxy for what climate change will
do across time.

*** D.3 Narrative tensions

What makes this region story-generative. The tensions that survive
translation into the valley's language. Each tension should be
nameable in a single phrase and expandable in a paragraph.

The existing Diamer survey has 9 named tensions. New surveys should
aim for at least 5.

** Part E: Sources, Gaps, and Fieldwork Needs

*** E.1 Consolidated sources

All URLs and citations gathered in one place, grouped by topic.
Sufficient for a reader to verify every claim. Use the project's
established format (inline sources per entity in Parts A–C, plus
consolidated list here).

*** E.2 Data gaps

What is known to be unknown. Use an org table:

#+begin_example
| Topic | Known | Missing | Priority |
|-------+-------+---------+----------|
| ...   | ...   | ...     | ...      |
#+end_example

Priority: /high/ (blocks Part C interpretation), /medium/ (would
strengthen the survey), /low/ (interesting but not critical).

*** E.3 Fieldwork needs

What can only be settled by being there. Not every gap can be closed
by web search or training knowledge. Some require walking the valley,
talking to the bitan, counting the ibex, smelling the juniper smoke.

* Per-Entity Entry Format

The project's established pattern for documenting a species, place,
or phenomenon within a survey:

1. Opening paragraph — taxonomic or general characterisation
2. *Physical* — morphological description, range, habitat
3. *Ecology near [place]* — behaviour in the specific survey region
4. *Cultural significance* — spirit, petroglyph, ritual connections
5. Language table (where relevant):
   =| Language | Name | Source |=
6. *Narrative interest* — what makes this entity story-generative
7. Sources — inline, immediately after the entry

Not every entry needs all sections. A fungal species may lack cultural
significance. A sacred grove may lack a morphological description. But
the skeleton is available.

* Confidence Annotation System

Embedded inline throughout the survey prose:

| Annotation | Meaning | Example |
|------------+---------+---------|
| =[cross-ref: filename.org]= | Grounded in existing verified project file | =[cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org]= |
| =[cross-ref: filename.org § Section]= | With section pointer | =[cross-ref: diamer-nanga-parbat.org § F.2]= |
| =[training-knowledge]= | From agent training data, not web-verified | After a factual claim without URL |
| =[needs-verification]= | Requires web search or fieldwork to confirm | Flags for the verification pass |

The verification pass upgrades =[training-knowledge]= to either
=[cross-ref:]= (found in project) or a cited source with URL. Items
that resist verification are promoted to data gaps (Part E).

** Status lifecycle

| Status | Meaning |
|--------+---------|
| =draft= | Written from training knowledge and cross-references |
| =draft-verified= | Web verification pass complete (URLs cited, facts checked) |
| =enriched= | Human has annotated from lived experience or fieldwork |

The =enriched= state is the goal. A survey that reaches =draft-verified=
is useful; a survey that reaches =enriched= is trustworthy.

* When to Split

A survey exceeding ~1500 lines should split into Part files in a
same-name subdirectory, following the multi-part output convention
(=develop/work-packages.org= §  Multi-Part Output Convention):

#+begin_example
domains/epistem/surveys/
  <slug>/
    <slug>.org              # master file with #+include directives
    part-a-construction.org
    part-b-configuration.org
    part-c-unravelling.org
    part-d-synthesis.org
    part-e-sources.org
#+end_example

Each Part file is self-contained: own title, own sources section.
The master file assembles them via =#+include= directives.

Surveys under 1500 lines remain a single file.
