* Purpose

This document is a second-order analysis: the review of reviews. It distils
the recurring patterns — structural, rhetorical, methodological — from the
corpus of review documents produced by the MāyāLucIA project. The goal is
not to summarise any single review, but to capture the /practice/ well
enough that an agent, given a domain and a question, could produce a review
in the project's established style.

The corpus comprises ten documents spanning four domains (AI evaluation,
neuroscience, geospatial/earth science, philosophy of human-machine
collaboration) and several genres (strategic briefings, technology surveys,
position papers, gap analyses, research papers, landscape surveys). The
diversity is the point: the patterns that recur /across/ these genres are
the ones that constitute the practice.

* 1. Document Architecture Patterns

Five recurring architectural moves constitute the skeleton of a review
document in this project. Not every document uses all five, but every
document uses at least three. The moves appear in a characteristic order,
though the order is sometimes rearranged for rhetorical effect.

** 1.1 The Opening: Problem or Gap, Not Introduction

The documents almost never open with a neutral introduction. Instead, they
begin with a /problem statement/ or a /gap identification/ — something is
wrong, missing, or newly urgent.

*** Pattern: The Universal Problem

State a structural problem that recurs across multiple domains, then assert
that nobody has solved it properly.

- DMT-for-AI-Agents opens: "Every domain where computational models compete
  faces the same structural problem" — then enumerates five symptoms.
- The Bravli landscape opens: "The question is not 'what can we build next?'
  but 'what can we /understand/ next?'" — reframing capability as inquiry.
- Cortical predictive coding opens with "The Accessibility Problem" — BBP's
  enterprise scale as a barrier to science.

*** Pattern: The Dead Predecessor

Acknowledge the death or failure of a prior project, then claim its ideas
survive.

- DMT-for-AI-Agents: "BBP is dead. But the ideas that emerged from it are
  precisely what the world needs now."
- DMT Project Charter: "BBP concluded in December 2024. The project and its
  institutional context are gone. But the architectural ideas..."
- Cortical predictive coding: "No individual scientist can reproduce this."
  (The BBP pipeline as a dead end for accessibility.)

*** Pattern: Why Now?

Three converging forces create urgency. The "why now" section typically
identifies 2–4 forces that have recently aligned to make the problem both
acute and solvable. These are not background — they are the timing argument
for action.

- DMT: EU AI Act (August 2026), NIST/ISO standardisation, AI weather model
  explosion.
- Autonomy position: Capability curve outpacing trust model, with the
  instruction-execution loop still dominant.
- Executable manuscripts: AI-native science (Sakana AI Scientist, ICLR 2025)
  creating a new category that nobody has properly filled.

** 1.2 The Survey: Landscape Mapping

Every document maps a landscape — sometimes explicitly (a table of 15+
tools), sometimes implicitly (a literature review that locates intellectual
ancestors). The survey is never neutral cataloguing; it is always oriented
toward revealing a gap.

*** Pattern: The Exhaustive Catalog with Structured Fields

For tool/framework surveys, enumerate every significant entry using a
consistent template. Each entry gets the same fields, enabling comparison.

- DMT-for-AI-Agents: 15 AI eval frameworks, each with: adapter pattern,
  report generation, non-LLM support, regulatory docs.
- GIS MCP review: 25+ MCP servers, each with: GitHub link, language,
  domain, key capabilities, Parbati relevance rating.
- Executable manuscripts: 10+ platforms, each with: plain text?, in-browser
  execution?, reactive?, agent-native?, multi-user?

The template varies by domain, but the principle is constant: /comparison
requires common dimensions/.

*** Pattern: Concentric Rings

For broader intellectual surveys, organise by increasing distance from the
project's core concern.

- Bravli landscape: Ring 1 (the fly connectome), Ring 2 (computational
  neuroscience methodology), Ring 3 (the reconstruction programme).
- Autonomy survey: Cybernetics → Pedagogy → AI Alignment → Anthropology of
  Knowledge → Gap Analysis.
- DMT-for-AI-Agents: AI/LLM (closest) → Weather → Finance → Pharma
  (furthest).

The rings create a natural arc from "what is immediately relevant" to "what
provides the deepest intellectual grounding." The reader can stop at any
ring and still have a coherent picture.

*** Pattern: The Genealogy

For intellectual traditions, trace a lineage — who begat whom, what was
preserved, what was lost.

- Executable manuscripts: Knuth (1984) → Jupyter (2014) → R Markdown/Quarto
  (2012–present) → Org-babel (2003–present), with explicit attention to
  what each descendant preserved or abandoned from Knuth's original vision.
- Autonomy position: Ashby (1956) → Pask (1975) → Beer (1972) → Bateson
  (1972), traced not chronologically but by conceptual dependency.
- Cortical predictive coding: Markram (2015) → MICrONS (2021) → Mathis Lab
  (2023–present), with BBP's curated recipes as the surviving scientific
  value.

The genealogy always carries a judgment about what survived and what was
lost. The Knuth → Jupyter lineage explicitly notes that Jupyter "abandoned"
noweb macros. The BBP → lazy-neuroscientist lineage explicitly claims that
the /recipes/ survive while the /pipeline/ is a dead end.

** 1.3 The Comparison: Summary Table + Prose

Every survey culminates in a comparison. The project has a strong preference
for /tables followed by prose interpretation/, not tables alone.

*** Pattern: The Gap Table

A comparison table whose final row is the proposed project, with a column
that shows what nobody else provides.

- DMT-for-AI-Agents: 11 frameworks × 5 dimensions. The "DMT (proposed)"
  row shows "Yes" in every column where competitors show "No."
- DMT Project Charter: same table, condensed.
- Executable manuscripts: 5 platforms × 7 capabilities, with Org-babel
  showing "Yes" where all others show "No" (noweb refs, 80+ languages).

The table is always followed by a single-sentence synthesis: "The gap is
clear: /no existing framework combines X with Y and Z./" This is the
document's thesis, compressed to one line.

*** Pattern: Cross-Domain Summary Table

When the survey spans multiple domains, a matrix showing the same
structural problem recurring in each domain.

- DMT-for-AI-Agents and Project Charter: Weather × Finance × Pharma ×
  AI/LLM, rows for "Multiple competing models," "Compare against reference
  data," "Regulatory documentation required," etc.
- Autonomy survey: "What has deep prior art" vs "What is genuinely new" —
  a different axis, but the same tabular structure.

*** Pattern: The "But" Clause

When discussing each predecessor or competitor in prose, the comparison
follows a template: /describe what they do well → "But" → identify what
they lack/. This is the paragraph-level unit of comparative analysis.

- Autonomy position §3: Each ancestor (Knight/Columbia, Bradshaw, CIRL,
  Constitutional AI, Lee & See) gets a paragraph describing its
  contribution, then a table or sentence explaining where it falls short.
- DMT-for-AI-Agents: Each framework gets bullet points for strengths,
  then a "*Limitation*:" line.
- Bravli landscape: Zhang et al. (2024) showed topology dominates. "This
  is profound — and suspicious." Then: known counterexamples.

** 1.4 The Positioning: Where We Stand

After the survey and comparison, the document explicitly positions the
project's own work. This is never buried — it gets its own section.

*** Pattern: What We Bring / What Carries Over

A section titled "What DMT Brings," "What We Bring," or "Where We Stand"
that distils the project's unique contributions into a short list.

- DMT: three capabilities (formal adapter pattern, structured reports,
  parameterised measurement).
- Autonomy position: five genuinely new contributions (bilateral
  negotiation, per-aspect granularity, machine-initiated de-escalation,
  audit trail as scientific record, non-propositional extension).
- Cortical predictive coding: the boundary-condition principle, with an
  explicit statement of what is gained and what is lost.

*** Pattern: Modernisation Table (What Was / What Will Be)

For projects that build on legacy work, a two-column table mapping old
components to their modern replacements.

- DMT: metaclasses → typing.Protocol, WithFields → dataclasses,
  Cheetah3 → Jinja2, Python 3.6 → 3.11+.
- Cortical predictive coding: BBP enterprise pipeline → lazy
  reconstruction; touch detection → statistical connectivity model.

*** Pattern: Independent Reinvention as Validation

A recurring rhetorical move: when multiple communities independently
arrive at the same architecture, that architecture has real structural
demand. This is not mere argument from authority — it is an argument from
convergent evolution.

- DMT: ProQSAR (drug discovery) reinvented DMT's modular pipeline.
  WP-MIP (weather) is building exactly what DMT provides. ValidMind
  (finance) is the SaaS version.
- Cortical predictive coding: Wilmes & Senn (2025) independently
  provided the computational framework that the boundary-condition
  approach needs.

** 1.5 The Close: Action Plan or Open Questions

Documents close either with a concrete action plan (for strategic
briefings) or with structured open questions (for landscape surveys and
position papers). Never a mere summary.

*** Pattern: The Phased Roadmap

For implementation-oriented documents, a time-phased plan with explicit
deliverables per phase.

- DMT-for-AI-Agents: Phase 1 (Core + AI, 6 weeks) → Phase 2 (Reports +
  Weather) → Phase 3 (Finance + Pharma) → Phase 4 (Ecosystem).
- DMT Project Charter: Phase 0 (Foundation) → Phase 1 → Phase 2 → Phase 3
  → Phase 4, with success criteria for each.
- Bravli landscape: Recommended Sequence, ordered by "scientific leverage
  per unit of implementation effort."

*** Pattern: The Big Questions

For research-oriented documents, a small number (2–4) of deep questions
that frame the entire programme.

- Bravli landscape: Q1 (Is the connectome sufficient to predict dynamics?),
  Q2 (What is the minimal model?), Q3 (What does a validated digital twin
  look like?).
- Autonomy position: Seven open questions (§10), each grounded in the
  literature reviewed.

*** Pattern: What Nobody Has Built Yet

A section explicitly naming the gap that the project intends to fill, in
concrete terms.

- Executable manuscripts: "What Nobody Has Built Yet" — a document where
  AI agents are native participants, not just producers or consumers.
- DMT: "The pattern is universal. The solution does not exist."
- Bravli: the five validation criteria that no neuroscience digital twin
  has satisfied.


* 2. Web Research Patterns

The corpus reveals a consistent approach to sourcing and integrating
external information. Four categories of external research recur.

** 2.1 Regulatory and Standards

Regulations are sourced for two purposes: to establish urgency ("why now")
and to define requirements ("what the framework must produce").

*** What gets cited

- EU AI Act: specific articles, enforcement dates, penalty structure.
- NIST TEVV: draft titles, release dates.
- ISO/IEC 42001: adoption status (e.g., "Microsoft requires it for AI
  vendors").
- SR 11-7 (Federal Reserve): specific requirements (three pillars).
- OECD QSAR guidelines: validation methodology requirements.
- Basel IV / FRTB: effective dates, implementation status.

*** How they're integrated

Regulations appear as concrete data points with dates, not as vague
context. The rhetorical move is: "Regulation X takes effect on [date]. It
requires [specific documentation]. /No existing tool produces this./" The
regulation is never presented as background — it is presented as a forcing
function.

*** Sourcing depth

The project goes to primary sources (official act text, NIST draft PDFs,
ISO standard descriptions) rather than secondary commentary. Regulatory
timelines are presented as tables with specific dates.

** 2.2 Academic Literature

Academic papers serve three functions: intellectual ancestry (who did this
before), validation data (what experimental results should we match), and
counterexamples (what breaks our assumptions).

*** Coverage standard

The project distinguishes between:

- *Foundational*: the seminal paper that introduced a concept. Always cited.
  (Brunel 2000 for LIF phase diagrams, Pask 1976 for conversation theory,
  Knuth 1984 for literate programming.)
- *Recent and relevant*: papers from the last 2–3 years that either validate
  or challenge the project's approach. Cited with explicit assessment.
  (Zhang et al. 2024, Wilmes & Senn 2025, Sakana AI Scientist 2025.)
- *Comprehensive in the target domain*: when surveying a field, the project
  aims for near-exhaustive coverage of significant contributions. The Bravli
  landscape cites 40+ papers; the autonomy survey cites 30+.

*** Integration style

Papers are never just listed — they always get a one-sentence assessment:
what they showed, what they missed, and what they mean for the project.
The pattern is: "Author (Year) showed X. /Implication/: Y." The
implication is always project-specific.

** 2.3 Tools and Products

For technology surveys, the project evaluates tools along consistent
dimensions.

*** What gets captured

- GitHub URL, stars, version, language
- Key capabilities (bullet list)
- Limitations (always explicit)
- Relevance to the project (rated: LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH / VERY HIGH)
- Pricing / licensing (when relevant)
- Active maintenance status (last release date)

*** Assessment criteria

The implicit hierarchy of values:
1. Does it have a formal adapter/interface pattern? (DMT surveys)
2. Does it produce structured output / reports? (DMT surveys)
3. Is it open-source? (Always preferred)
4. Does it work with plain text / version control? (Manuscript surveys)
5. Does it have an active community? (Stars, contributors, release cadence)

The project is allergic to dashboards. "Dashboards are not documentation"
is a thesis that appears in DMT-for-AI-Agents, the Project Charter, and
implicitly in the executable manuscripts survey.

** 2.4 Market and Timing

"Why now" arguments are constructed from three ingredients:

1. *Regulatory deadlines*: Hard dates that create compliance pressure.
2. *Technology proliferation*: When a domain suddenly has 5+ competing
   models (AI weather models, LLMs, QSAR platforms), the need for
   standardised comparison becomes acute.
3. *Funding signals*: VC rounds (Curvenote $1.4M), enterprise
   partnerships (ValidMind/Experian), government initiatives (WP-MIP)
   that prove market demand exists.

The timing argument always has a "but" — the demand is real, but the
solution is absent. "Whether the market exists is an open question"
(Executable manuscripts survey, on Curvenote) — the project is honest
about market uncertainty even when making a bullish case.


* 3. Comparison Methodology

** 3.1 Recurring Comparison Dimensions

The dimensions vary by domain, but a meta-pattern is visible: every
comparison asks the same four structural questions.

| Question | In DMT reviews | In neuroscience reviews | In philosophy reviews |
|----------+----------------+-------------------------+----------------------|
| What does it do? | Capabilities list | What it showed | What it claims |
| What can't it do? | Limitations | What it hides/misses | What it assumes |
| How does it relate to us? | Gap vs our approach | Implication for bravli | Where we depart |
| Is it actively maintained? | Stars, version, date | Citation count, recency | Living tradition? |

The first two questions are descriptive. The third is always
project-relative — the comparison is never neutral. The fourth is a
pragmatic filter.

** 3.2 When Tables, When Prose

The project follows a clear (if implicit) rule:

- *Tables* for feature comparison across 5+ items with discrete
  (yes/no/partial) values. Tables are always followed by a prose
  synthesis sentence.
- *Prose with "but" clauses* for comparing 2–4 items along complex
  dimensions. Each item gets a paragraph.
- *Nested tables* for cross-domain comparison (rows = pattern, columns =
  domain). These appear at the synthesis level, after the per-domain
  surveys.

Tables never stand alone. They are always interpreted.

** 3.3 Handling "Closest Existing Work"

The autonomy position paper §3 is the model: each ancestor gets a
structured comparison.

1. One paragraph describing the prior work's contribution.
2. A table showing specific dimensions of comparison (6-column table:
   Aspect | Prior Work | Our Approach).
3. A synthesis sentence: "We adopt X and add Y."

This structure recurs in:
- DMT vs lm-eval-harness (adapter pattern comparison)
- Cortical predictive coding vs BBP pipeline (accessibility comparison)
- Org-babel vs Jupyter/Quarto (capability comparison table)

** 3.4 Relationship Framing

The project's relationship to compared work is framed in one of four ways:

| Relationship | Framing | Example |
|--------------+---------+---------|
| Successor | "We modernise / rebuild" | DMT (2017) → DMT-eval (2026) |
| Orthogonal | "We solve a different problem" | DMT vs dashboarding tools |
| Complement | "We fill the gap they leave" | Lazy reconstruction + BBP recipes |
| Independent convergence | "They reinvented our approach" | ProQSAR, WP-MIP, ValidMind |

The project never frames its work as a /competitor/ to prior work. It is
always positioned as solving a /different/ or /deeper/ problem.


* 4. Voice and Epistemic Stance

** 4.1 Uncertainty Expression

The project uses calibrated language with a distinctive vocabulary:

| Confidence level | Typical phrasing |
|------------------+------------------|
| Established | "X is the case," "the pattern is universal" |
| Likely | "This is consistent with," "the evidence suggests" |
| Uncertain | "an open question," "genuinely unknown," "whether X is Y remains unclear" |
| Speculative | "this is a bet," "the key question is whether" |
| Acknowledged ignorance | "we cannot test yet," "no one has achieved this" |

The phrase "genuinely unknown" recurs (Bravli landscape, autonomy
position). It signals that the uncertainty is not hedging but a real gap
in knowledge. "An open question" is the most common uncertainty marker.

The project explicitly separates known / inferred / speculated in the
CLAUDE.md instructions ("Epistemic Hygiene"), and this discipline is
visible in the reviews. Speculative claims are always flagged.

** 4.2 Person and Register

*** First person

"We" appears in two modes:
- *Project "we"*: "We propose," "We argue," "Our approach." Used in
  position papers and the DMT documents. This is the project speaking
  with a unified voice.
- *Investigative "we"*: "We ask," "We test," "We can probe this."
  Used in the Bravli landscape and MB dynamics paper. This is the
  researchers planning their inquiry.

"I" never appears. The project is always collaborative.

*** Impersonal

Used for describing external work: "The framework provides," "Brunel
(2000) classifies," "Markram et al. (2015) demonstrated."

*** The directive register

A distinctive feature: instructions directed at the future self or at
agents. "The project should keep the DMT identity." "We should design
every simulation with explicit falsification conditions." "The machine
should have a disposition that captures the essence." This is the project
talking to itself across time.

** 4.3 Ratio of Reporting to Judgment

The ratio varies by document type:

| Genre | Reporting : Judgment |
|-------+----------------------|
| Tool survey (GIS MCP) | 80:20 — mostly description, with relevance ratings |
| Strategic briefing (DMT) | 50:50 — equal parts survey and argument |
| Position paper (autonomy) | 30:70 — mostly argument grounded in prior work |
| Landscape (bravli) | 40:60 — questions and implications dominate |
| Research paper (MB dynamics) | 60:40 — methods and results, then interpretation |

The project /always/ judges. Even the most descriptive survey (GIS MCP
review) ends every entry with a relevance assessment. Pure description
without evaluation does not appear anywhere in the corpus.

** 4.4 Domain Expertise Surfacing

The human's (mu2tau's) background surfaces through:

- *Physics vocabulary*: "phase diagram," "boundary conditions," "stochastic
  resonance," "Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process." Used naturally, never explained.
- *BBP institutional knowledge*: "The BBP closed at EPFL in December 2024,"
  "7 years building and using DMT," specific recipe file paths.
- *Indian epistemological tradition*: pramāṇa theory (pratyakṣa, anumāna,
  śabda, upamāna), guru-śiṣya paramparā, rāga/gamaka/meend. Integrated
  as living intellectual resources, not exotic decoration.
- *Statistical physics sensibility*: comfort with universality arguments
  ("the same pattern recurs across every domain"), phase transitions,
  scaling, ensemble thinking.

The expertise is never displayed for its own sake. It appears precisely
when it illuminates the argument — the physics training enables the
boundary-condition approach to reconstruction, the BBP years ground the
DMT architecture claims, the Indian tradition provides the epistemic
framework that the autonomy agreement needs.

** 4.5 Characteristic Rhetorical Moves

*** The Profound-and-Suspicious

Present a strong result, then immediately question it.

- "This is profound — and suspicious." (Zhang et al. topology-dominates)
- "This is technically impressive but niche." (Nextjournal)
- "Whether the market exists is an open question." (Curvenote)

This move maintains intellectual honesty while engaging with the material.
It prevents sycophantic acceptance of claimed results.

*** The One-Sentence Thesis

After extended analysis, compress the finding to one line.

- "The gap is clear: no existing framework combines a formal model-agnostic
  adapter pattern with structured report generation and non-LLM support."
- "The pattern is universal. The solution does not exist."
- "Nobody else is building this stack."
- "A connectome is not enough."

*** The Structural Aphorism

Short, quotable statements that encode a design principle.

- "Dashboards are not documentation."
- "BBP is dead. But the ideas that emerged from it are precisely what
  the world needs now."
- "There is no correct model. But some may be useful."
- "The connectome defines the space of possible circuits; neuromodulation
  selects from that space."

These carry the project's philosophy in compressed form.


* 5. Integration with the Project Cycle

The project's core cycle is Measure → Model → Manifest → Evaluate → Refine.
Reviews connect to this cycle in specific ways.

** 5.1 Reviews as Measure

The primary function of reviews is /measurement of the landscape/ — what
exists, what is missing, where the project sits. This is the Measure phase
applied not to natural phenomena but to the intellectual and technological
environment.

Every review begins by measuring: the GIS MCP review measures 25+ tools,
the DMT survey measures 15+ frameworks, the Bravli landscape measures the
space of open questions. The measurement is always structured — dimensions
are defined before data is gathered.

** 5.2 Reviews as Evaluate

The secondary function is /evaluation of the project's own work/ against
what exists. This is explicit in the positioning sections: "What DMT
Brings," "Where We Stand," "What We Gain and Lose."

The evaluation is always relative: not "is our work good?" but "how does
our work compare to what exists, and where are we uniquely positioned?"

** 5.3 Temporal Relationship to Building

Reviews appear in three temporal positions relative to building:

| Position | Example | Function |
|----------+---------+----------|
| Before building | GIS MCP review → Parbati tooling choices | Inform design decisions |
| During building | Bravli landscape → research programme design | Guide priorities |
| After building (partially) | DMT survey → reborn framework design | Position and justify |

The DMT documents are particularly interesting: they review existing
frameworks /after/ having built and used the original DMT for 7 years. The
review is retrospective positioning — "we already solved this; now we show
that the world needs it."

** 5.4 Reviews Feed Commissioning

Reviews are the evidential basis for commissioning decisions:

- The GIS MCP review → Parbati tooling stack recommendation.
- The DMT survey → project charter and phased roadmap.
- The autonomy survey → spirit ecology questions (WP-0003).
- The Bravli landscape → recommended research sequence.

The review produces a map; the commissioning decision says "go here."


* 6. The Practice, Distilled

An agent producing a review document in this project's style should follow
this procedure.

** 6.1 Before Writing: Scope and Framing

1. *Identify the genre*: strategic briefing, tool survey, literature
   survey, position paper, gap analysis, or some combination. Most
   documents in this project are /combinations/ — a tool survey that
   becomes a position paper, a literature review that becomes a gap
   analysis.

2. *Define the question*: What is the review trying to answer? Not "what
   is out there?" but "what is out there /that matters for us/, and what
   is missing?"

3. *Choose comparison dimensions*: Before surveying, decide the 4–6
   dimensions along which items will be compared. These dimensions should
   be project-relevant (not just technically descriptive).

4. *Decide the ring structure*: What is closest to the project's core
   concern? What provides broader context? Organise from inner ring
   (most relevant) to outer ring (deepest grounding).

** 6.2 The Survey Phase

5. *Exhaustive within scope*: For the inner ring, aim for near-complete
   coverage. For outer rings, be selective but cite the foundational work.

6. *Consistent template per entry*: Every item in the survey gets the
   same fields. For tools: name, URL, language, capabilities, limitations,
   relevance rating. For papers: author, year, key finding, implication.

7. *Primary sources*: Go to the original paper, the official regulation
   text, the GitHub repo. Not secondary commentary.

8. *Capture what's missing*: The gap is more important than the catalog.
   Note absences explicitly.

** 6.3 The Comparison Phase

9. *Build the gap table*: A comparison matrix with the project's work as
   the final row. The table should make the gap visually obvious.

10. *Write the "but" clauses*: For each significant predecessor, describe
    its contribution, then identify what it lacks. Be specific: "no
    structured reports" not "insufficient reporting."

11. *Name the relationship*: successor, orthogonal, complement, or
    independent convergence. Never competitor.

12. *Cross-domain matrix*: If the review spans domains, build a second
    table showing the same structural pattern recurring in each domain.

** 6.4 The Positioning Phase

13. *Distil to 2–4 unique contributions*: What does the project bring
    that nobody else has? Be concrete and specific.

14. *The one-sentence thesis*: Compress the entire review's finding into
    a single declarative sentence.

15. *Acknowledge what is lost*: The modernisation table, the
    gains-and-losses section. Never claim pure improvement — name the
    tradeoffs.

** 6.5 The Close

16. *Action plan or open questions*: Never end with a summary. End with
    what to do next (phased roadmap) or what remains unknown (structured
    open questions).

17. *Feed the next phase*: Explicitly connect the review's findings to
    commissioning decisions, tool choices, or research priorities.

** 6.6 Throughout

18. *Calibrated uncertainty*: Separate known from inferred from
    speculated. Use "genuinely unknown," "an open question," "this is a
    bet." Never paper over uncertainty with fluent prose.

19. *Always judge*: Never pure description. Every entry, every section,
    every survey item gets an assessment — relevance, quality, gap.

20. *Structural aphorisms*: When a finding is both important and
    compressible, state it as a single memorable sentence.

21. *Tables + interpretation*: Tables never stand alone. Always follow
    with a prose synthesis.

22. *The profound-and-suspicious*: When encountering a strong claim,
    engage with it seriously and then question it. What are the
    counterexamples? When does it break?

23. *Org-mode format*: Source of truth in =.org=. Structured headings.
    Tables in org table syntax. Code in source blocks. Citations inline.


* 7. Anti-Patterns

What the project's reviews do /not/ do, and should continue to avoid.

1. *Neutral cataloguing*: Never list without judging. A catalog that does
   not reveal a gap is not a review — it is a bibliography.

2. *Uncalibrated enthusiasm*: Never claim something is "revolutionary" or
   "game-changing." The project prefers: "this is the most comprehensive
   available" or "this independently validates the architecture."

3. *Dashboard worship*: The project is constitutionally skeptical of
   dashboards, GUIs, and cloud platforms. The preference is for plain
   text, version control, structured documents, and open-source tools.

4. *Ungrounded speculation*: Speculation is allowed (see uncertainty
   vocabulary) but must be flagged. A claim about "what the market needs"
   must be grounded in regulatory deadlines, funding signals, or
   independent reinvention — not wishful thinking.

5. *Ignoring what is lost*: Every positioning move that claims an
   advantage must also name the tradeoff. "What we gain and lose" is
   not optional.

6. *Summary conclusions*: "In conclusion, this survey has examined..."
   is not a conclusion. The close must be forward-looking — what to
   build, what to investigate, what remains unknown.


* 8. Notes for Phase 2 (Spirit Commissioning)

The patterns extracted here suggest the following for the review spirit's
identity and powers:

** Disposition

The review practice is fundamentally about /locating yourself on a map
you've built/. Two metaphors from the WP suggest themselves:

- *trace-the-watershed*: follow the flow of ideas to their source.
  Genealogical. Emphasises the lineage-tracing aspect of the practice.
- *read-the-strata*: read the layers of prior work that the present
  rests on. Geological. Emphasises the depth of survey.

Both capture something essential. The practice combines horizontal sweep
(comprehensive catalog) with vertical depth (intellectual genealogy).

** Powers

Based on the extracted patterns, the review spirit would need:

1. *Landscape survey*: exhaustive tool/paper cataloguing with consistent
   templates.
2. *Gap identification*: the ability to find what is /absent/ in a
   landscape, not just what is present.
3. *Comparative analysis*: structured comparison along project-relevant
   dimensions, producing gap tables.
4. *Positioning*: locating the project's work relative to the surveyed
   landscape.
5. *Synthesis*: compressing extended analysis into structural aphorisms
   and one-sentence theses.
6. *Web research*: sourcing primary regulatory documents, academic
   papers, GitHub repos, and market signals.

** Guild

The epistem guild (resolved by WP-0001). The review practice is
structured knowledge work — the methodical acquisition, organisation,
and evaluation of what is known.

** Module

The aikosh module (resolved by WP-0001). The review tooling becomes
part of aikosh's collection machinery — schemas for review entries,
query patterns for gap analysis, consistency checks across reviews.
