* The Question

Humanity has decided to bet its remaining civilizational resources on
massive AI infrastructure. Hyperscale data centers consume gigawatts,
drain aquifers for cooling, and concentrate computational power in the
hands of a few corporations in a few geographies. All serious
investigations indicate this is not sustainable. But it is not even
clear what the bet is — what, exactly, are we building all this
compute /for/?

Meanwhile, an energy revolution is unfolding in the Global South: a
solar-powered future, distributed, modular, closer to the people it
serves.

This experiment asks: *Is it feasible to run algorithmically enhanced
intelligence on silicon chips without wrecking the biosphere?*

Specifically: can a network of micro-data-centers, distributed along a
mountain river — powered by micro-hydro and solar, cooled by glacial
water — provide meaningful computation while remaining within
ecological limits? Not as a toy. As a provocation with numbers behind
it.

** Why a Mountain River

A Himalayan river like the Parvati offers three things hyperscale
cannot:

1. *Continuous energy gradient* — water falls ~4000m over ~90km, a
   natural engine. Micro-hydro (run-of-river, no dam) taps this
   without interrupting the flow.
2. *Free thermal management* — glacial meltwater at 4-8°C is the
   world's best coolant. The river absorbs waste heat and carries it
   downstream, diluted.
3. *Distributed topology* — the valley is linear, settlements strung
   along the river. Computation can follow the same pattern: small
   nodes at each settlement, connected by fiber or radio, forming a
   valley-scale distributed system.

** Why This Is Also Dangerous

The Parvati Valley has already suffered from hydropower. Large dams
have wrecked hydrological flow, displaced communities, and fragmented
ecosystems. "Micro" and "green" are not automatically benign. Thermal
discharge into a cold river can devastate aquatic life even at small
scales. Run-of-river intakes can strand fish and alter sediment
transport. Roads built for maintenance scar slopes.

This experiment must hold both possibilities: that distributed
micro-computation /could/ coexist with mountain ecosystems, and that
it /could/ be yet another extractive technology dressed in green
clothing. The numbers will tell us which.

* Larger Context

** The Hyperscale Bet

- As of 2025-2026, global data center power consumption is projected
  to reach 1000+ TWh/year by 2030 (IEA estimates)
- Single hyperscale facilities: 100-500 MW each (equivalent to a
  small city)
- Water consumption: millions of liters/day for evaporative cooling
- Geographic concentration: Virginia, Oregon, Ireland, Singapore,
  Middle East — all facing local resource conflicts
- The bet: that the economic returns from AI will justify this
  infrastructure. But the returns are speculative while the resource
  consumption is certain

** The Solar South

- India's solar capacity exceeded 80 GW in 2025, growing ~15 GW/year
- Cost of solar has fallen below coal in most Indian states
- Distributed solar microgrids are reaching villages that never had
  grid power
- The pattern: energy production moving from centralized to
  distributed, from fossil to renewable, from Global North to Global
  South

** The Synthesis We Are Testing

What if computation followed the same trajectory as energy? From
centralized hyperscale to distributed micro-scale. From fossil-cooled
to naturally-cooled. From resource-extractive to
ecosystem-integrated. Not as ideology — as engineering with an
ecological budget.

* Approach

We proceed in three layers, each building on the last:

** Layer 1: Physical Feasibility Model

Estimate, with real numbers for the Parvati Valley:

- *Energy yield*: Given river gradient, flow rates (seasonal), and
  micro-hydro efficiency — how many watts per kilometer of river?
  Supplement with solar insolation data for the valley.
- *Thermal capacity*: Given water temperature and flow rate — how many
  watts of waste heat can the river absorb before thermal discharge
  becomes ecologically harmful? (The binding constraint.)
- *Compute yield*: Given available power and cooling — how many
  GPU-equivalents (or inference operations/second) can the valley
  sustain? Compare to a single hyperscale facility.
- *Network topology*: What connectivity is feasible between
  micro-nodes in a valley with limited infrastructure?

Key data sources:
- SRTM DEM for elevation/gradient (already have N32E077.hgt)
- CWC / BBMB river flow data for the Parvati
- IMD solar radiation data for Kullu district
- Published thermal tolerance data for Himalayan aquatic species

** Layer 2: Ecological Impact Analysis

For the physical configuration determined above:

- *Thermal plume modeling*: How does waste heat disperse in a mountain
  river with turbulent, cold flow? How far downstream before
  temperatures return to baseline?
- *Aquatic ecosystem constraints*: What are the thermal tolerances of
  key indicator species (snow trout, macroinvertebrates)? What is the
  maximum allowable temperature delta?
- *Sediment and flow*: Do run-of-river intakes alter sediment
  transport or create fish barriers?
- *Cumulative impact*: One micro-DC is benign. What about 20 along
  90km? Where are the tipping points?
- *Comparison*: How does the ecological footprint of valley-distributed
  micro-compute compare to the footprint of an equivalent hyperscale
  facility (including its share of grid fossil generation and water
  consumption)?

** Layer 3: Digital Twin / Visualization

Make the analysis tangible:

- A simplified digital twin of the Parvati Valley showing:
  - River profile with gradient and flow
  - Micro-DC placement options (at settlements, at gradient
    inflection points)
  - Power generation potential (color-coded along the river)
  - Thermal plume extent downstream of each node
  - Ecological constraint zones (spawning grounds, protected areas,
    GHNP boundaries)
- Interactive: adjust number of nodes, compute load per node, see
  ecological impact change
- The twin makes the tradeoffs /visible/ — not just numbers in a
  table but a landscape you can read

* What We Expect to Learn

- An order-of-magnitude estimate: how much computation can a single
  Himalayan valley sustain within ecological limits?
- Whether the binding constraint is energy, cooling, or ecology
- How valley-scale distributed compute compares to hyperscale — not
  as a replacement, but as a calibration of what "sustainable AI
  infrastructure" actually means
- Whether the solar + micro-hydro + natural cooling combination
  changes the economics enough to matter
- A visual/narrative artifact that makes these tradeoffs legible to
  non-specialists

* What We Are Not Claiming

This is not a business plan, a policy proposal, or an engineering
blueprint. It is a thought experiment with numbers — a provocation
grounded in physics rather than ideology. The Parvati Valley is a
concrete case study, not a deployment target.

If the numbers say it doesn't work, that is an equally valuable
result: it means there is no ecologically sustainable path to
large-scale AI inference, and the hyperscale bet is consuming the
biosphere for a speculative return.

* Data Assets

** DEM — Himachal Pradesh full coverage

12 SRTM 1-arc-second tiles covering 30-33°N, 75-79°E, stitched into a
single 10801×14401 array (155 million pixels at ~30m resolution).

- Elevation range: 159m (Punjab plains) to 6751m (Kinnaur peaks)
- Zero voids
- Stored as =data/himachal_dem.npz= (204 MB, gitignored)
- Re-downloadable via =fetch_dem.py=

** Dams — structured dataset

49 hydroelectric projects in =dams.csv=, covering all five river
basins (Sutlej, Beas, Ravi, Chenab, Yamuna). Fields: name, river,
basin, lat, lon, type, capacity (MW), status, operator.

Summary by basin:

Sources: CEA, HPPCL, NHPC, SJVN, BBMB records; Wikipedia; Global
Energy Monitor. Coordinates approximate (±1-10 km) for smaller
projects.
